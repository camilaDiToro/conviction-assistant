# CRIs and CRAs: A Guide to Brazilian Securitized Bonds

## Certificados de Recebíveis Imobiliários and Certificados de Recebíveis do Agronegócio

---

## Introduction

Among the most distinctive instruments in the Brazilian fixed income landscape are the CRI (Certificado de Recebíveis Imobiliário) and the CRA (Certificado de Recebíveis do Agronegócio). These securitized credit instruments combine the structural sophistication of international asset-backed securities with Brazil's unique regulatory framework and tax advantages. For individual investors, they are among the few fixed income instruments that offer income tax exemption on earnings — a meaningful benefit in a country with relatively high marginal tax rates.

Understanding CRIs and CRAs requires familiarity with Brazilian securitization mechanics, the originator ecosystem, credit evaluation methodologies, and the specific risks these instruments carry compared to simpler alternatives like CDBs or Tesouro Direto.

---

## What Are CRIs and CRAs?

### CRI — Certificado de Recebíveis Imobiliário

A CRI is a fixed income security backed by real estate receivables — streams of future payments arising from real estate transactions. These receivables may include:

- Mortgage installments from home purchasers
- Commercial real estate lease payments
- Receivables from property sale contracts (contratos de compra e venda)
- Receivables from built-to-suit or sale-leaseback structures

CRIs are issued exclusively by securitization companies (Companhias Securitizadoras de Créditos Imobiliários) regulated by CVM. They are not issued by banks. The issuing securitization company purchases the receivable pool from the originator and issues certificates backed by that pool.

### CRA — Certificado de Recebíveis do Agronegócio

A CRA is the agricultural equivalent, backed by receivables arising from agribusiness activities. Eligible underlying receivables include:

- Payments owed by farmers to input suppliers (seeds, fertilizers, pesticides)
- Receivables from rural credit operations
- Export crop advance payments (CPR — Cédula de Produto Rural)
- Receivables from agribusiness companies selling to domestic or export markets

CRAs are issued by specialized agribusiness securitization companies (Companhias Securitizadoras de Créditos do Agronegócio) under the regulatory framework established by Law 11,076/2004.

---

## Legal Basis

**CRI legal framework**: Law 9,514/1997 established the legal foundation for CRIs, creating the securitization company structure and defining eligible real estate receivables. Subsequent CVM instructions and CMN resolutions have refined the regulatory requirements over time.

**CRA legal framework**: Law 11,076/2004 created the legal framework for agribusiness receivable certificates, alongside related instruments like the CPR (Cédula de Produto Rural) and the LCA (Letra de Crédito do Agronegócio).

Both instruments operate under the broader supervision of the CVM and the Banco Central do Brasil, with ANBIMA (Associação Brasileira das Entidades dos Mercados Financeiro e de Capitais) playing a self-regulatory role in setting standards for issuance and disclosure.

## Key Differences Between CRI and CRA

| Feature | CRI | CRA |
|---|---|---|
| Underlying Receivables | Real estate transactions | Agribusiness transactions |
| Legal Basis | Law 9,514/1997 | Law 11,076/2004 |
| Eligible Originators | Real estate developers, banks, retailers with property receivables | Agricultural producers, input suppliers, cooperatives, agribusiness companies |
| Typical Collateral | Real estate liens, fiduciary assignment of receivables | Agricultural products, rural property liens, crop pledges |
| Market Size | Larger, more mature market | Rapidly growing, particularly post-2015 |
| Predominant Index | IPCA, CDI, IGP-M | IPCA, CDI, USD (for export-linked receivables) |

## Eligible Originators

### CRI Originators

The originating entities that sell real estate receivables into CRI structures include:
- Real estate developers (incorporadoras) — the largest originator category
- Commercial banks with mortgage portfolios
- Retailers offering seller financing on real estate
- Companies with large commercial property lease receivables (e.g., large retailers, logistics operators)

A critical regulatory distinction: CRI funds must flow to real estate-related activities. CVM has increasingly scrutinized "CRIs de bolso" — structures where large corporations issue CRIs nominally tied to real estate receivables but with the economic purpose of simply raising cheap capital. Regulatory changes in 2023 (CVM Resolution 60) tightened the nexus requirements between the receivables and genuine real estate activities.

### CRA Originators

Eligible CRA originators include:
- Agricultural producers (rural producers, either individual or corporate)
- Agricultural cooperatives
- Agribusiness input companies (fertilizer, seed, pesticide companies)
- Agribusiness companies with export receivables
- Companies operating in the food processing chain with agricultural input receivables

The agribusiness supply chain generates an enormous volume of commercial credit — farmers routinely purchase inputs on credit, with payment due at harvest. These receivables are the natural feedstock for CRA structures.

## The Securitization Process

Understanding how a CRI or CRA is structured is essential for evaluating its risks:

**Step 1 — Receivable Generation**: The originator creates or accumulates receivables through its business activities (mortgage origination, agricultural input sales on credit, commercial lease agreements).

**Step 2 — Assignment to Securitization Company**: The originator assigns (transfers) the receivable pool to a securitization company (Companhia Securitizadora). This assignment is typically done under fiduciary alienation (alienação fiduciária) or via cessão fiduciária, providing the securitization company with a priority claim on the receivables.

**Step 3 — Creation of a Patrimônio Separado**: The receivables are ring-fenced into a separate patrimony (patrimônio separado), legally isolated from the securitization company's own balance sheet. This is the crucial investor protection mechanism — even if the securitization company goes bankrupt, the receivable pool backing the CRI/CRA is isolated from its general creditors.

**Step 4 — Issuance of Certificates**: The securitization company issues CRI or CRA certificates in tranches (séries), typically differentiated by seniority, maturity, and yield. Senior tranches receive priority in both payment waterfall and loss absorption. Subordinated tranches absorb losses first.

**Step 5 — Appointment of Agente Fiduciário**: A trustee (agente fiduciário) is appointed to represent investor interests throughout the life of the instrument. The trustee monitors covenant compliance, receives receivable collections, and acts on behalf of investors in case of default or restructuring.

**Step 6 — Distribution to Investors**: Certificates are distributed to investors through capital markets, either via public offerings (with CVM registration) or restricted placements (ICVM 476 / CVM Resolution 160 for qualified investors).

**Step 7 — Ongoing Collections and Distribution**: Receivable collections flow through the patrimônio separado and are distributed to certificate holders according to the payment waterfall, typically monthly or semi-annually.

## Tax Exemption for Individual Investors

One of the most commercially significant features of CRIs and CRAs is their income tax exemption for individual investors (pessoas físicas). Under Brazilian tax law:

- Interest income and capital gains from CRIs and CRAs are **exempt from income tax** for individual investors
- This exemption does not apply to legal entities (pessoas jurídicas), which pay standard IRPJ/CSLL on investment income
- The exemption applies to both the periodic coupon payments and any gain realized upon sale in the secondary market

The practical effect of this exemption is significant. A CRI yielding CDI + 1.5% is equivalent, on an after-tax basis, to a CDB yielding CDI + 3.0% or more (depending on the investor's marginal tax bracket and the applicable IOF/IR table for the CDB).

This tax advantage has driven enormous retail investor appetite for CRIs and CRAs, contributing to rapid market growth and, in some periods, compression of spreads to levels where the risk-adjusted return advantage over taxable alternatives has diminished.

## Credit Risk in CRI and CRA Structures

Despite the tax advantage and securitized structure, CRIs and CRAs carry meaningful credit risk that investors must evaluate carefully.

### Originator Risk vs. Structural Credit Enhancement

The credit quality of a CRI or CRA depends on the quality of the underlying receivable pool, not merely the creditworthiness of the issuing securitization company. However, many CRIs and CRAs in the Brazilian market — particularly those from large corporate originators — are effectively corporate credit instruments in securitized form. The receivable pool may consist of payments owed by (or guaranteed by) a single large company, making the credit analysis similar to unsecured debenture analysis.

### Types of Credit Risk

**Debtor Risk**: The risk that the underlying debtors (mortgagors, agricultural purchasers on credit) fail to make their scheduled payments. In diversified pools (many small debtors), this risk is partly diversifiable. In concentrated pools (one large corporate obligor), it is not.

**Originator Risk**: Some structures include performance obligations by the originator (e.g., servicing the receivables, substituting defaulted receivables from a reserve pool). If the originator deteriorates financially, these obligations may not be met.

**Structural Risk**: Legal challenges to the validity of the assignment, defects in the fiduciary pledge, or challenges to the patrimônio separado structure could theoretically undermine investor protections, though Brazilian courts have generally upheld these structures.

**Prepayment Risk**: Receivables may be paid earlier than scheduled, forcing reinvestment at potentially lower rates. This is more relevant for CRIs backed by mortgage pools where borrowers may refinance.

## Rating Requirements and Collateral Structures

### Credit Ratings

Not all CRIs and CRAs carry formal credit ratings, but for publicly offered instruments targeting retail investors, ratings from ANBC-registered agencies (Fitch, Moody's, S&P, and domestic agencies like Austin Rating) are common. Ratings evaluate both the credit quality of the underlying receivables and the structural features that provide credit enhancement.

Credit enhancement mechanisms in CRI/CRA structures include:

- **Subordination**: Senior tranches are protected by subordinated tranches that absorb first losses
- **Overcollateralization**: The face value of receivables in the pool exceeds the face value of issued certificates, providing a buffer against defaults
- **Reserve Funds**: Cash reserves accumulated from excess spread to cover temporary payment shortfalls
- **Originator Guarantees**: Some structures include credit guarantees from the originator or third-party sureties (fiadores), though these introduce originator risk back into the credit analysis
- **Real Property Liens**: For CRIs, real estate collateral under fiduciary alienation provides a foreclosure recovery path

### Collateral Coverage Requirements and Regulatory Standards

Market participants and rating agencies routinely require overcollateralization ratios as a condition of rating assignment. In typical senior/subordinated structures, the senior tranche may benefit from collateral coverage of 110-130% of outstanding principal, with subordinated tranches providing additional first-loss protection below the senior threshold.

As per CMN Resolution 4,734, CRIs and CRAs must maintain a minimum collateral coverage ratio of 150% of outstanding principal throughout the life of the instrument. Senior tranches require a minimum of 120% coverage while subordinated tranches must maintain 180% to compensate for their first-loss position. The ratio is verified monthly by the trustee (agente fiduciário) and reported to CVM. Instruments falling below the required coverage trigger an automatic cure period of 30 days, after which the trustee may call an investor assembly to approve restructuring measures.

## How to Evaluate CRIs and CRAs

A rigorous evaluation framework for these instruments covers several dimensions:

### 1. Receivable Pool Quality
- What is the credit profile of the underlying debtors?
- Is the pool diversified or concentrated in a single obligor?
- What is the historical delinquency and recovery experience for similar pools?
- Are there covenants requiring pool substitution if quality deteriorates?

### 2. Structural Analysis
- What are the seniority and waterfall provisions?
- What is the overcollateralization ratio, and how does it behave under stress scenarios?
- Is the patrimônio separado truly ring-fenced? Have the assignment documents been reviewed by independent legal counsel?
- What are the triggers for accelerated repayment or early amortization?

### 3. Originator/Servicer Analysis
- If the originator is also the servicer, what happens in case of originator default?
- Does the originator have a track record in originating similar receivables?
- Are there backup servicer arrangements?

### 4. Spread Analysis
- What is the nominal yield relative to comparable-duration CDI-linked or IPCA-linked benchmarks?
- After adjusting for the tax exemption (add back the tax equivalent for comparability), is the spread adequate for the credit and liquidity risk?
- How does the spread compare to similarly rated debentures?

### 5. Liquidity Profile
- Is there an active secondary market for this specific instrument?
- What is the bid-ask spread in the secondary market?
- Are there any lock-up periods or redemption restrictions?

## Secondary Market Liquidity

Secondary market liquidity for CRIs and CRAs is one of the more significant practical concerns for investors. Unlike Tesouro Direto bonds — which have a guaranteed buyback mechanism through the Tesouro Nacional — CRIs and CRAs depend entirely on market-making by brokers and banks.

Liquidity varies dramatically across instruments:
- **Highly liquid**: Large-deal CRIs from major corporate issuers (Petrobras, Embraer, large agribusiness companies) with broad investor bases
- **Moderate liquidity**: Mid-market deals with R$200-500 million issuance size, traded among qualified investors
- **Illiquid**: Small issuances from local developers or regional agribusiness companies, effectively held to maturity by the initial buyer

Investors who may need to liquidate before maturity should focus on larger, more widely distributed issuances and check recent secondary market trading volumes before investing.

## FIDC as a Related Instrument

The FIDC (Fundo de Investimento em Direitos Creditórios) is a closely related instrument that often appears alongside CRIs and CRAs in investor discussions. A FIDC is an investment fund that holds credit receivables directly, rather than issuing certificates backed by them.

Key differences:
- FIDCs are funds (with fund structure, regulations, quota holders' meetings) while CRIs/CRAs are securities
- FIDC quotas are not tax-exempt for individual investors
- FIDCs provide easier diversification across many receivable types within a single fund
- Some FIDCs hold CRIs or CRAs as part of their portfolio

For retail investors seeking diversified exposure to the credit receivables market with smaller minimums, FIDC-of-FIDCs or funds that invest in CRIs and CRAs may provide practical access with lower per-instrument concentration risk.

## Risks Summary

| Risk Type | Description | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Credit Risk | Underlying debtors default | Pool diversification, overcollateralization, reserves |
| Prepayment Risk | Early repayment at unfavorable times | Lockout periods, prepayment penalties in underlying contracts |
| Liquidity Risk | Inability to sell before maturity at fair price | Focus on larger, widely distributed issues |
| Originator Risk | Originator failure disrupts servicing | Backup servicer, true sale structures |
| Legal/Structural Risk | Challenges to patrimônio separado | Legal opinions, track record of structure type |
| Interest Rate Risk | Mark-to-market losses if rates rise | Match duration to investment horizon |
| Regulatory Risk | Changes to tax exemption or eligibility rules | Diversify across instrument types |

## How to Access CRIs and CRAs

### Direct Investment
Individual investors can purchase CRIs and CRAs directly through their brokerage accounts on B3's primary and secondary markets. The minimum investment varies by issuance — some are accessible from R$1,000, while others require R$1 million or more (restricting them to qualified or professional investors under CVM definitions).

ANBIMA maintains a database of publicly registered issuances. Investors should review the offering memorandum (prospecto) before investing, paying particular attention to the pool description, credit enhancement structure, and risk factors section.

### Investment via Funds
Several fund categories provide exposure to CRIs and CRAs:
- **Fundos de Renda Fixa Crédito Privado**: May hold CRIs/CRAs as part of a broader credit portfolio
- **Fundos de CRI/CRA**: Dedicated funds with concentrated exposure to these instruments, offering professional receivable pool selection and monitoring
- **FIDCs**: As discussed above, related but structurally distinct

Fund-based access provides diversification and professional management but introduces a layer of fees and may not fully preserve the tax efficiency (fund-level income may be taxable depending on fund structure).

---

## Conclusion

CRIs and CRAs occupy a distinctive niche in the Brazilian fixed income market: they combine the structural complexity and potential yield premium of securitized credit instruments with an income tax exemption that significantly enhances their after-tax returns for individual investors. This combination has made them increasingly mainstream, moving from an institutional-only instrument to a broadly accessible retail product.

The key discipline for investors is to see through the tax benefit to the underlying credit quality. A well-structured CRA backed by a diversified pool of creditworthy agricultural receivables with appropriate overcollateralization is fundamentally different from a poorly disclosed single-obligor structure dressed up in CRA form. Evaluation rigor — on the pool, the structure, the originator, and the price — remains the essential skill for capturing the genuine opportunity these instruments represent.
