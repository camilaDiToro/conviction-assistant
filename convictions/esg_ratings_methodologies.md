# ESG Rating Methodologies Applied to Brazilian Companies

*Decade Investment Research | Internal Conviction Document*

---

## Overview

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) analysis has become an integral component of Decade's equity and fixed income research process. Brazil presents a distinctive ESG landscape: the country's economy is deeply intertwined with natural resources (agriculture, mining, oil and gas), governance structures often reflect concentrated family or state ownership, and social challenges — income inequality, land rights, informal labor — are among the most complex of any major economy. Standard ESG frameworks developed by global rating agencies require meaningful local calibration to be analytically useful in the Brazilian context.

---

## Major ESG Rating Providers Active in Brazil

**MSCI ESG Ratings** is the most widely referenced agency among institutional investors globally. MSCI covers approximately 150 Brazilian listed companies and assigns ratings from AAA to CCC based on industry-specific Key Issue weights. MSCI's methodology emphasizes "management of ESG risks" rather than absolute impact: a company operating in a high-risk sector (oil and gas, mining) can achieve an 'A' rating if its risk controls are strong relative to peers, even if its absolute environmental footprint is large. Petrobras, for example, has oscillated between 'BB' and 'BBB' on MSCI's scale, reflecting governance improvements following the Lava Jato corruption investigations weighed against persistent operational environmental risks.

**Sustainalytics** (owned by Morningstar) measures "ESG Risk" in absolute terms rather than relative to peers. Sustainalytics scores companies from 0 (negligible risk) to 100+ (severe risk), with Brazilian commodity companies typically scoring in the 30–50 range (high risk). The distinction matters: MSCI's peer-relative approach can show Brazilian miners as "average" within their sector, while Sustainalytics' absolute approach highlights the elevated inherent risk of the activity.

**ISS ESG** (Institutional Shareholder Services) provides ratings used primarily by European institutional investors and proxy advisors. ISS ESG is particularly influential in governance scoring, given ISS's dominant role in proxy advisory services. Its governance assessments of Brazilian companies focus on board composition, related-party transactions, and dual-class share structures — all areas where many Brazilian issuers have historically shown weaknesses.

**Local providers** include Sitawi Finance for Good (governance and social metrics for mid-cap Brazilian companies not covered by global agencies) and the Fundação Getúlio Vargas (FGV) research groups that produce thematic ESG studies. These are supplementary rather than primary sources for Decade's quantitative ESG integration.

## Rating Divergence Between Agencies

A well-documented challenge in ESG investing is the low correlation between agency ratings — estimated at 0.3–0.6 across major agencies globally (Berg, Koelbel, Rigobon, 2022, Review of Finance). This divergence is amplified for Brazilian companies due to:

- **Scope differences**: MSCI covers deforestation in supply chains (relevant for meatpackers, soy traders); Sustainalytics may weight direct operations more heavily.
- **Data availability**: Brazilian small and mid-caps have limited ESG disclosure, leading agencies to apply sector-average proxies that produce divergent results.
- **Governance interpretation**: State-owned enterprise governance is interpreted differently; some agencies penalize government control as a governance risk, others treat it as a form of implicit accountability.

Decade triangulates across at least two agency ratings for any position with material ESG considerations, supplemented by proprietary engagement and primary data collection where available.

## B3's ISE Index (Índice de Sustentabilidade Empresarial)

The ISE, managed by B3 with methodology developed in partnership with FGV-EAESP, is the primary domestic ESG benchmark for Brazilian companies. Launched in 2005, the ISE selects approximately 30–40 companies annually from the universe of the 200 most liquid B3 stocks, based on a self-assessment questionnaire covering five dimensions: economic-financial, social, environmental, governance, and general/nature of the product.

Crucially, ISE membership is voluntary: companies opt in by completing the questionnaire. This creates selection bias — companies with weaker ESG profiles self-select out. The ISE is therefore better interpreted as a "best-in-class" screen among willing participants than as a comprehensive ranking of B3 liquidity. Since its inception, the ISE has shown performance broadly comparable to the Ibovespa, with periods of modest outperformance during stress events linked to governance scandals (Lava Jato in 2015–2016, Americanas in 2023), reflecting the downside protection from governance screens.

## Unique Brazilian ESG Challenges

**Deforestation and land rights** are the most material and internationally scrutinized ESG risks for Brazilian companies. Meatpackers (JBS, Marfrig, Minerva), soy traders (Amaggi, Bunge Brasil), and timber companies face traceability requirements under the Amazon Soy Moratorium (2006), the Brazilian Forest Code (Lei 12.651/12), and increasingly under European supply chain due diligence regulations (EU Deforestation Regulation — EUDR, effective 2025). Decade's analysis of agribusiness companies includes proprietary monitoring of supply chain deforestation indicators via satellite data providers (MapBiomas, Global Forest Watch).

**Governance in state-owned companies** is a structural challenge. SOE boards in Brazil are subject to political appointment, creating risk of decisions that prioritize electoral or fiscal objectives over shareholder value. The Petrobras governance reforms post-Lava Jato (independent board members, internal audit strengthening, anti-corruption compliance program) are a positive example, but the risk of political interference in pricing and investment decisions has not been eliminated, as demonstrated by executive turnover during political transitions.

**Informal labor and supply chain social risk** affects retailers, construction companies, and agribusiness. Brazil's labor inspectorate (Ministério do Trabalho) publishes the "Lista Suja" — a registry of employers found to have subjected workers to conditions analogous to slavery. Inclusion on this list triggers procurement blacklists and financial penalties. Decade screens all portfolio company supply chains against the Lista Suja as a minimum standard.

## How Decade Integrates ESG Ratings

ESG analysis at Decade is integrated at two levels:

**Screening**: Companies rated CCC by MSCI or scoring above 50 on Sustainalytics' risk scale are flagged for additional review before inclusion in client mandates with ESG constraints. ISE exclusion for companies that choose to participate but fail the questionnaire triggers a governance risk review.

**Valuation adjustment**: Material ESG risks are reflected in the discount rate (adding 0.5–2.0% to WACC for companies with elevated environmental or governance risks), in probability-weighted scenario analysis (e.g., cost of potential deforestation-linked trade sanctions for agribusiness), and in qualitative narrative within conviction documents.

## Limitations of ESG Scores and Greenwashing Risks

ESG ratings have significant limitations that investors must internalize:

- **Backward-looking data**: Most ESG scores are based on disclosed historical data, not forward-looking risk assessment.
- **Self-reported inputs**: Companies control significant portions of the data that feed ESG scores, creating incentives for selective disclosure.
- **Greenwashing**: Brazil has seen a proliferation of "green" commitments without credible implementation plans. Embraer, major banks, and several retailers have announced net-zero targets with timelines extending to 2040–2050, where accountability is minimal. Decade applies a greenwashing filter assessing whether intermediate milestones (2030 Scope 1/2 reduction targets) are credible, science-based (aligned with SBTi — Science Based Targets initiative), and accompanied by capital allocation commitments.

The CVM issued guidance (Ofício Circular CVM/SSE 01/2023) warning against greenwashing in ESG-labeled funds and products, signaling growing regulatory attention to the quality of ESG claims in the Brazilian capital markets.

## ESG Integration in Fixed Income: Green and Social Bonds

Brazil's green bond and social bond market has grown substantially since the first Brazilian corporate green bond (BRF, 2015). The CVM regulates sustainable bonds through CVM Resolution 59/2021, requiring alignment with ICMA (International Capital Market Association) principles or equivalent frameworks.

Key considerations for Decade's fixed income analysis:

- **Use of proceeds verification**: Green bonds must allocate proceeds to eligible projects (renewable energy, sustainable buildings, clean transportation). Decade reviews prospectus frameworks and post-issuance allocation reports.
- **External review**: Second-party opinions (SPOs) from providers such as Vigeo Eiris, Sustainalytics, or SITAWI provide independent validation of green bond frameworks. SPO quality varies; Decade gives weight to reviews aligned with ICMA's four-pillar framework.
- **Pricing premium (greenium)**: Brazilian green bonds have historically priced at a modest greenium of 5–15 bps tighter than equivalent conventional bonds from the same issuer. This spread compression is lower than observed in European markets, reflecting relatively lower demand from domestic ESG-mandated investors.

Social and sustainability-linked bonds (SLBs) — where the coupon is tied to achievement of ESG KPIs (e.g., reduction in GHG emissions by a specified date) — require careful analysis of KPI ambition. Decade has observed SLBs with step-up penalties of only 12.5–25 bps for missing targets, which may be insufficient to create genuine incentives for issuer behavior change.
