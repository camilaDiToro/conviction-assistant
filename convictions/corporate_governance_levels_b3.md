## Executive Summary

B3's tiered corporate governance listing architecture — spanning from the baseline Tradicional segment through Nível 1, Nível 2, and the flagship Novo Mercado — represents one of the most impactful market structure innovations in Brazilian capital market history. The governance listing levels were designed to address a well-documented problem: Brazil's historically weak minority shareholder protections, the prevalence of dual-class share structures concentrating control with founding families and the state, and the information asymmetry between controlling and minority investors.

For Decade advisors and their clients, understanding governance listing levels is not merely a compliance exercise — it is central to fundamental equity analysis. Companies in higher governance segments have empirically demonstrated lower cost of capital, greater institutional investor participation, better liquidity, and — critically — lower frequency of value-destroying related-party transactions and sudden governance failures. This document provides a comprehensive treatment of each listing level, the investor protections they provide, the valuation premium associated with stronger governance, and the methodologies for assessing governance quality beyond the listing level label.

## Historical Context: Why B3 Created the Governance Tiers

The governance listing program was launched by the then-Bovespa exchange in December 2000, motivated by a specific market failure. Despite Brazil's growing economic importance, foreign institutional investors systematically avoided or underweighted Brazilian equities relative to their fundamental valuations. The primary deterrent was not macroeconomic risk — it was the structural disadvantage imposed on minority shareholders by the prevailing corporate governance regime.

Under the Companies Act in force at the time (and largely preserved in Lei 6.404/76), controlling shareholders could issue preferred shares (ações preferenciais, PNs) without voting rights, concentrating voting control in common shares (ações ordinárias, ONs) held by the controlling group while raising capital from the public through PNs. This structure enabled controlling shareholders to extract value through related-party transactions, below-market asset transfers, and excessive management compensation — with minority shareholders holding economically significant stakes but no practical voting power to resist.

The governance listing levels attacked this problem through a voluntary contractual framework: companies that chose to list on higher governance segments committed to specific protections for minority shareholders, with enforcement through mandatory arbitration at B3's Câmara de Arbitragem do Mercado (CAM). This opt-in design respected private ordering while creating a credible signaling mechanism — only companies with genuinely strong governance were willing to commit to the enhanced obligations.

## The Four Listing Levels

### Tradicional (Traditional)

The baseline listing level imposes only the minimum requirements of Lei 6.404/76 and CVM regulations. Companies listed in the Tradicional segment may:
- Issue preferred shares (PN) with no voting rights.
- Maintain dual-class share structures with concentrated voting control.
- Provide tag-along rights at 80% of the price paid to the controlling shareholder in a change of control (the legal minimum under Article 254-A of Lei 6.404/76).
- Have no minimum free float requirement beyond CVM registration thresholds.
- Resolve disputes through the Brazilian court system (judiciary) rather than arbitration.
- Have boards composed with no specific independence requirement beyond CVM minimum rules.

**Investor implications**: Tradicional-listed companies command the highest equity cost of capital from sophisticated investors and the lowest valuation multiples in equivalent business quality comparisons. The absence of enforced minority protections creates a structural "governance discount" — rational investors demand higher expected returns to compensate for the agency risk premium.

Many Tradicional-listed companies are legacy listings from the pre-2000 era, state-controlled entities, or companies with controlling families unwilling to dilute control through a governance upgrade. Some genuinely well-run companies remain in this segment due to inertia or specific structural reasons, and advisors should not treat Tradicional listing as an automatic disqualifier — but it requires deeper governance investigation.

### Nível 1

The first voluntary governance tier introduces meaningful improvements over Tradicional, focused primarily on disclosure and liquidity:

**Key Nível 1 requirements**:
- **Minimum free float**: 25% of total shares outstanding.
- **Enhanced quarterly disclosure**: Publication of standardized quarterly financial statements (ITR — Informações Trimestrais) with specific footnote disclosures regarding related-party transactions, shareholder agreements, and capital structure.
- **Annual calendar**: Companies must publish a calendar of corporate events (dividend payments, AGMs, material disclosures) at the beginning of each year.
- **Annual investor meeting (APIMEC meeting)**: Companies must host a formal annual meeting with investment analysts and institutional investors.
- **Tag-along rights**: 80% for ON shares (same as legal minimum); PN shareholders begin receiving some tag-along protection in practice, though Nível 1 does not mandate full tag-along parity.

**Investor implications**: Nível 1 adds some disclosure discipline and minimum liquidity without fundamentally altering the share structure or minority voting rights. It is a meaningful step up from Tradicional but leaves the most important governance issues — dual-class voting, arbitration preference, board independence — unaddressed. Many large-cap Brazilian financial institutions (banks, insurance companies) are listed at Nível 1.

### Nível 2

Nível 2 represents a substantial qualitative leap, addressing voting rights and dispute resolution in ways that Nível 1 does not:

**Key Nível 2 requirements**:
- **Voting rights for preferred shareholders**: PN shareholders are granted voting rights on specific critical matters (merger approvals, change of control transactions, related-party transactions above specified thresholds). This partially addresses the structural disadvantage of PN holders without requiring conversion to a single class.
- **Tag-along at 100% for all shareholders**: Both ON and PN shareholders receive 100% tag-along rights in a change of control — meaning minority shareholders must receive the same price per share (on an economically equivalent basis) as the controlling shareholder in any acquisition.
- **Board independence**: A minimum of 20% of board members must be independent directors as defined by B3 rules (no material relationship with the company, management, controlling shareholder, or significant supplier/customer).
- **Mandatory arbitration**: Disputes between the company, management, controlling shareholders, and minority shareholders must be resolved through B3's Câmara de Arbitragem do Mercado (CAM), bypassing the Brazilian court system (which is slower and less specialized in corporate governance matters).
- **Annual financial statements in English**: Or in a format accessible to international investors, easing foreign institutional participation.
- **Audit committee recommendation**: Functionally required through disclosure obligations, though formal audit committee mandate is stronger at Novo Mercado.

**Investor implications**: Nível 2 is particularly valuable for protecting PN shareholders — a significant constituency in the Brazilian market. The mandatory arbitration requirement deserves special attention: CAM arbitration has proven substantially more effective at enforcing minority shareholder rights than the Brazilian judiciary, with faster timelines (typically 18–24 months vs. 7–10 years for court proceedings) and arbitrators with capital markets expertise.

### Novo Mercado

Novo Mercado is the pinnacle of B3's governance architecture, representing a global benchmark for emerging market corporate governance. Its central distinguishing feature is the **mandatory single-class share structure**: all companies listed on Novo Mercado must issue exclusively ordinary voting shares (ONs), with no preferred shares permitted. This structural change — eliminating dual-class voting — is the most powerful governance protection available, as it aligns voting rights perfectly with economic ownership.

**Key Novo Mercado requirements**:

**Share structure**:
- Only ordinary voting shares (ON) permitted. No preferred shares without voting rights.
- Exception: Limited issuance of non-voting shares for employee incentive plans (participações) within specified thresholds.

**Free float**:
Novo Mercado requires a minimum free float of 30% of total shares outstanding, measured quarterly. Companies falling below this threshold for two consecutive quarters enter a 12-month compliance period before potential delisting proceedings. This requirement was raised from the original 25% in the comprehensive 2018 Novo Mercado regulation reform, which also introduced the mandatory arbitration chamber requirement.

**Board composition**:
- Minimum of 5 board members.
- At least 20% must be independent directors (effective independence as defined by B3's detailed criteria, which exclude anyone with a family, professional, or financial relationship with management or controlling shareholders).
- Board terms are limited to two years, renewable.
- CEO and Board Chairman must be separate individuals (no dual role permitted).

**Tag-along rights**:
- 100% tag-along for all shareholders in any change of control transaction — identical to Nível 2.

**Disclosure**:
- Annual financial statements in English or IFRS-compatible format.
- Quarterly earnings releases within specified deadlines.
- Material fact (fato relevante) disclosure with same-day requirements.
- Annual reference form (Formulário de Referência) with comprehensive corporate, governance, and related-party disclosure.

**Mandatory arbitration**:
All disputes must go to B3's CAM — same as Nível 2 but with a broader scope covering all parties (including minority shareholders vs. other minority shareholders in corporate restructuring contexts).

**Audit committee (Comitê de Auditoria)**:
Novo Mercado companies must have either: (a) a permanent fiscal board (Conselho Fiscal) activated as an audit committee equivalent, or (b) a formal audit committee meeting specified independence and expertise requirements.

**Change of control tender offer (OPA por saída)**:
Upon delisting from Novo Mercado for any reason, the controlling shareholder must conduct a tender offer for all remaining shares at fair value (determined by independent valuation), protecting minority shareholders from being stranded in a less liquid, less protected structure.

## Governance Levels and Investor Value: The Valuation Premium

Academic and practitioner research on Brazilian equities consistently documents a "governance premium" — higher valuation multiples for companies in higher governance segments, controlling for size, sector, and earnings quality. The intuition is straightforward: investors are willing to pay more for earnings streams that are less vulnerable to expropriation by controlling shareholders.

Key findings from the Brazilian governance premium literature:

- **Novo Mercado vs. Tradicional**: Studies have documented average P/E and EV/EBITDA premiums of 20–40% for Novo Mercado-listed companies versus comparable Tradicional-listed companies in equivalent sectors and size cohorts.
- **Liquidity differential**: Novo Mercado companies consistently attract a broader institutional investor base, particularly foreign investors, which improves secondary market liquidity and further reduces the equity cost of capital.
- **Cost of equity**: The governance premium corresponds mechanically to a lower cost of equity capital — a 1–2 percentage point discount on required return translates, at reasonable terminal growth rates, into the documented 20–40% premium in enterprise values.
- **Dividend sustainability**: Novo Mercado companies, because minority shareholders have genuine voting power and arbitration access, are less likely to have dividends redirected through related-party channels. The distributions that reach public shareholders are more reliable.

**Practical implication for Decade advisors**: When evaluating equity opportunities across governance segments, the governance discount in Tradicional-listed companies should be explicitly modeled, not simply noted as a qualitative risk. A company trading at 8x P/E in the Tradicional segment should be compared to Novo Mercado peers at 12x P/E with recognition that the differential reflects, in part, a rational governance risk adjustment.

## The IGC Index (Índice de Governança Corporativa)

B3 maintains the **IGC (Índice de Ações com Governança Corporativa Diferenciada)**, a market capitalization-weighted index comprising companies listed exclusively in the Novo Mercado, Nível 2, and Nível 1 segments. The IGC serves as:

- A performance benchmark for the universe of governance-differentiated companies.
- An eligibility gateway for governance-focused equity funds.
- A signal to the market about the composition of B3's highest-governance tier.

B3 publishes the Índice de Governança Diferenciada (IGD) annually, scoring all listed companies from 0 to 100 based on 47 governance criteria covering board independence, minority shareholder protections, disclosure quality, and capital structure. Companies scoring above 75 qualify for inclusion in the IGC (Índice de Governança Corporativa) and receive a 15% discount on B3's annual listing fees as an incentive for governance improvement.

The IGC has historically outperformed the Ibovespa over long measurement periods, which is consistent with the hypothesis that governance quality correlates with superior long-run risk-adjusted returns. However, the relationship is not uniform across cycles — governance-differentiated companies can underperform during speculative cycles where investors favor high-beta, lower-quality names, and outperform during risk-off periods when governance quality acts as a value preservation mechanism.

## Assessing Governance Quality Beyond the Listing Level

Listing level provides a regulatory minimum standard, not a comprehensive governance guarantee. Companies within the same listing level can vary dramatically in their actual governance practices. Advisors must look beyond the label.

### Board Composition Analysis

**Beyond the 20% independence threshold**: The minimum 20% board independence required by Nível 2 and Novo Mercado is a necessary but insufficient condition for board effectiveness. Advisors should examine:
- **Director profiles**: Are independent directors genuinely independent — or are they connected to the controlling family through social networks, past employment, or business relationships that fall outside the technical definition of non-independence?
- **Board diversity**: Board diversity (gender, professional background, international experience) correlates with more rigorous oversight. All-male boards dominated by former company insiders warrant scrutiny.
- **Director tenure**: Very long-tenured "independent" directors gradually develop relationships and dependencies that compromise genuine independence. Best practice sets a maximum of 10 years before independence designation lapses.
- **Committee structure**: A functioning audit committee with genuine financial expertise (at least one member with accounting/audit background) and a compensation committee that sets management pay independently of the CEO are markers of board quality.

### Related-Party Transactions (RPTs)

Related-party transactions are the most common mechanism of minority shareholder value transfer in Brazilian corporate governance. Key red flags:
- Transactions between the company and entities controlled by the controlling shareholder at non-arm's-length prices.
- Management compensation structures that include below-market loans, company-subsidized personal expenses, or undisclosed benefits.
- Asset sales to the controlling group below independently appraised values.

Brazilian regulation requires disclosure of all material RPTs in the Reference Form (Formulário de Referência). Advisors should review the RPT section of the Reference Form annually for the companies in client portfolios, with particular attention to RPTs involving real estate, consulting services, or financing — areas where pricing manipulation is most difficult to detect.

### Shareholder Agreements (Acordos de Acionistas)

Shareholder agreements filed with the CVM govern voting behavior among controlling shareholders and can effectively concentrate decision-making in ways not apparent from the formal capital structure. B3 requires filing of material shareholder agreements. Key questions:
- Does the agreement give any party disproportionate blocking rights or veto powers over operational decisions?
- Are there tag-along waiver provisions that could disadvantage minority shareholders in a future change of control?
- Does the agreement include drag-along provisions that could force minority shareholders to sell under unfavorable conditions?

### Poison Pill Provisions

Many Brazilian companies — including some in the Novo Mercado segment — have adopted **poison pill** provisions in their bylaws that impose punitive takeover obligations on any shareholder who acquires above a specified threshold (typically 20–35% of total shares). The structure typically requires a mandatory tender offer for the entire remaining free float at a price premium (often 100–150% above the 30-day average price) as a deterrent to hostile acquisitions.

The governance implications are mixed:
- **Positive**: Can protect minority shareholders by ensuring any change of control involves a premium offer.
- **Negative**: Entrenches existing management and controlling shareholders, potentially destroying value by preventing beneficial control transfers that could improve company performance.
- **Novo Mercado tension**: Some poison pill structures in Novo Mercado companies create de facto multiple voting classes — the shareholder who triggers the pill is structurally penalized while the controlling group avoids the trigger through pre-existing holdings.

Advisors should evaluate whether a specific company's poison pill aligns with or undermines minority investor interests, rather than treating all poison pills uniformly as either governance positive or negative.

## Recent Governance Controversies in Brazil

Brazilian corporate governance has generated high-profile cases that illustrate both the power and the limits of the listing level framework:

**Americanas (AMER3)** — 2023: The discovery of BRL 43 billion in "accounting inconsistencies" at one of Brazil's largest retailers — Lojas Americanas, a Novo Mercado-listed company with a seemingly strong governance profile — demonstrated that listing level protections cannot substitute for accounting integrity and genuine audit committee oversight. The case revealed how board composition that appears compliant on paper can still fail to detect or prevent massive financial fraud, particularly when management is sophisticated and the controlling shareholders are highly involved operationally.

**Samarco / BHP / Vale (post-2015)**: The Mariana dam collapse and its aftermath exposed how environmental, social, and governance risks in extractive industries can create liabilities orders of magnitude larger than any governance compliance failures. This case accelerated the integration of ESG factors into Brazilian institutional investment frameworks.

**Oi Telecom bankruptcy**: Oi's protracted bankruptcy proceeding illustrated how capital structure complexity (multiple classes of debt, cross-shareholdings, RPTs with related infrastructure entities) can systematically disadvantage minority bond and equity holders even within a regulated framework.

These cases do not argue against the governance listing framework — they argue for treating it as a necessary but insufficient condition for governance quality, and for supplementing listing level analysis with continuous fundamental governance review.

## ESG and Governance: The Institutional Investor Intersection

Corporate governance (the "G" in ESG) has become a central pillar of institutional investor due diligence in Brazil, driven by:

- **Foreign institutional ownership**: MSCI and other global index providers include governance quality screens in their emerging market methodology. Companies perceived as governance-weak face systematic underweighting by passive international capital.
- **Brazilian institutional investor engagement**: ABRAPP (Brazilian Association of Pension Funds) and PREVIC (pension fund regulator) have strengthened governance requirements for investee companies, including expectations around board independence and RPT disclosure.
- **B3's ESG initiatives**: B3 provides ESG data feeds and collaborates with index providers on ESG-integrated index construction, creating commercial incentives for listed companies to improve governance disclosures.

**Governance as ESG alpha**: Decade's view is that governance quality is the most directly investable ESG dimension for a fundamental equity investor. Environmental and social factors, while material over long horizons, are difficult to price with precision. Governance quality — measurable through board composition, RPT frequency and terms, capital allocation track record, and dividend policy — translates into quantifiable cash flow protection for minority shareholders.

## Voting Rights: ON vs. PN Shares

The distinction between ordinary (ON) and preferred (PN) shares is at the center of Brazilian governance analysis:

- **ON shares (ações ordinárias)**: Full voting rights at all shareholder meetings. Holders can vote on all matters, including election of board members, approval of related-party transactions, mergers, and dividend policy.
- **PN shares (ações preferenciais)**: Historically no voting rights in exchange for priority in dividend payments (and sometimes a dividend premium, typically 10% above ON dividends). However, if the company fails to pay dividends for three consecutive years, PN shares acquire full voting rights until distributions resume.

For Novo Mercado companies, this distinction is moot — only ONs exist. For Nível 2 companies, PN holders have restricted but meaningful voting rights on specific decisions. For Tradicional and Nível 1 companies, PN holders are economically exposed to company performance but structurally disadvantaged in governance participation.

**Practical implication**: When advising clients on whether to hold the ON or PN class of a Tradicional or Nível 1 company, advisors should consider the governance control value of the ON class against any dividend premium attached to the PN class. In companies where a change of control is plausible, the ON class typically commands a premium (because it carries tag-along rights in control transfers that may or may not accrue to PN holders under minimum-compliance frameworks).

## Governance Trends in Brazil

The trajectory of Brazilian corporate governance has been broadly positive since 2000, with notable acceleration since the Lava Jato investigations (which, whatever their procedural controversies, dramatically elevated awareness of corporate corruption and governance failure). Key trends:

**1. Novo Mercado migration**: The share of B3's total market capitalization represented by Novo Mercado companies has grown significantly since 2000. IPOs have been overwhelmingly on Novo Mercado since the 2004–2008 IPO boom, meaning the composition of the market is structurally shifting toward better governance.

**2. Stewardship codes**: AMEC (Associação de Investidores no Mercado de Capitais) and ANBIMA have developed stewardship principles encouraging institutional investors to exercise voting rights actively and engage with investee companies on governance matters.

**3. Director liability**: Brazilian courts have increasingly held board members personally liable for governance failures, increasing the incentive for independent directors to exercise genuine oversight.

**4. Digital AGMs**: The 2020–2022 period normalized digital participation in Annual General Meetings, dramatically increasing retail shareholder participation in governance voting — a structural improvement in minority shareholder engagement.

**5. Gender diversity**: The CVM has introduced enhanced disclosure requirements around board gender composition, and several governance indices now include gender diversity screens. The pipeline of female board directors in Brazil is growing, though representation at the board chair and lead independent director level lags.

## Conclusion

B3's corporate governance listing levels represent a foundational analytical framework for Brazilian equity investing — not a checkbox compliance exercise. The empirical evidence is clear: companies in higher governance segments command valuation premiums, attract more institutional capital, generate better long-run risk-adjusted returns, and offer minority shareholders meaningful legal protections through arbitration that courts cannot provide with equivalent speed or expertise.

For Decade advisors building equity portfolios, governance listing level should be an explicit screening and weighting criterion. The Novo Mercado universe should be the starting point for any equity analysis; excursions into Nível 2, Nível 1, or Tradicional companies require explicit acknowledgment of the additional agency risk being assumed and a commensurate return premium embedded in the expected return analysis.

Beyond the listing level label, continuous governance monitoring — board composition, RPT analysis, shareholder agreement review, poison pill assessment — is the standard of care that sophisticated institutional analysis demands. The 2023 Americanas case demonstrated, with brutal clarity, that governance compliance can coexist with governance failure. The label is a necessary but not sufficient condition.

Decade's investment team integrates governance quality analysis throughout the fundamental research process, treating the governance discount/premium as a valuation factor alongside earnings growth and balance sheet quality. Advisors who internalize this framework will provide superior risk-adjusted guidance to their clients over full market cycles.

### II. Historical Governance Reform Timeline in Brazil: 2000 to Present

**December 2000 — Launch of Novo Mercado and Governance Tiers**
Bovespa launched the voluntary governance tier system (Novo Mercado, Nível 2, Nível 1) as a radical market structure innovation. Initial uptake was modest — only a handful of companies immediately migrated. The prevailing view among Brazilian controlling shareholders was that enhanced minority protections imposed unnecessary constraints on operational flexibility.

**2002–2004 — First Wave of Governance-Level IPOs**
The 2001–2002 period saw the first companies conduct IPOs directly on Novo Mercado (including Natura Cosméticos in 2004, which became a landmark case of a governance-friendly family-controlled company accessing capital markets at premium valuations). This signaled to the market that the governance premium was real and accessible.

**2004–2008 — IPO Boom and Novo Mercado Adoption Surge**
Brazil's commodity supercycle, combined with the Lula government's macroeconomic stability, triggered the largest IPO boom in Brazilian history — over 120 companies went public between 2004 and 2008. Driven by investor demand, virtually all of these IPOs were conducted on Novo Mercado. This structural shift rapidly changed the composition of the Brazilian equity market toward higher governance.

**2008–2010 — Global Financial Crisis and Governance Test**
The global financial crisis tested Brazilian corporate governance. Several companies demonstrated that Novo Mercado listing did not prevent poorly timed capital raises (secondary offerings at depressed prices), leveraged acquisitions, or management compensation excesses. However, the most egregious related-party abuses were largely absent from Novo Mercado companies, validating the basic framework.

**2009 — CVM Resolution on Related-Party Transactions (ICVM 480 and 481)**
The CVM modernized its disclosure regime, requiring detailed related-party transaction disclosure in the newly created Reference Form (Formulário de Referência). This enhanced transparency framework became central to minority shareholder analysis.

**2010 — Lava Jato Precursor Events: Panamericano Crisis**
Banco Panamericano's discovery of a R$ 4 billion accounting fraud (concealed by double-selling receivable portfolios to multiple financial institutions) was an early warning of the risks of inadequate audit quality. The intervention, managed by FGV and BACEN, did not involve a listed equity issuer per se, but catalyzed regulatory attention to accounting quality.

**2013–2015 — Operation Lava Jato (Car Wash) and Its Market Impact**
The federal anticorruption investigation initiated in March 2014 exposed systematic bribery schemes involving Petrobras, major engineering contractors (Odebrecht, OAS, Andrade Gutierrez), banks, and political parties. The investigation obliterated tens of billions of reais in equity market capitalization and crystallized awareness among institutional investors that corporate governance was a genuine alpha factor. Governance-focused managers — those who had underweighted state-linked contractors and Petrobras — significantly outperformed.

**2017 — Brazilian Corporate Governance Code (Código de Governança Corporativa)**
IBGC (Instituto Brasileiro de Governança Corporativa) and CVM jointly promoted a new voluntary Brazilian Corporate Governance Code, based on the "comply or explain" principle from UK corporate governance tradition. Listed companies began disclosing annually their adherence to the Code's recommendations, creating a structured mechanism for governance gap identification.

**2018 — Novo Mercado Comprehensive Rule Reform**
B3 undertook the most significant revision of Novo Mercado rules since the 2000 launch. Key changes included: raising minimum free float from 25% to 30%, mandatory separation of CEO and Board Chair roles, minimum 5-member boards, enhanced audit committee requirements, and expanded CAM arbitration scope. The reform was implemented after extensive market consultation and introduced stricter enforcement mechanisms.

**2019 — CVM Resolution on Executive Compensation Disclosure**
The CVM required listed companies to disclose individual executive compensation details — highest, average, and lowest paid executive — ending a practice of aggregate-only disclosure. This transparency measure increased investor ability to evaluate pay-for-performance alignment and identify excessive compensation.

**2020–2021 — Digital AGMs and Retail Shareholder Participation**
The COVID-19 pandemic forced the CVM to authorize digital Annual General Meetings (AGMs) in early 2020. The innovation proved durable — shareholder participation rates increased dramatically, with retail investors representing a meaningful voting bloc for the first time in many companies. B3 and the CVM made digital AGM participation permanent, representing a structural democratization of governance participation.

**2023 — Americanas Collapse and Governance Reckoning**
The January 2023 revelation of BRL 43 billion in "accounting inconsistencies" at Americanas (AMER3) — a Novo Mercado company with three of Brazil's wealthiest families as controlling shareholders — prompted the most significant governance debate in Brazil since Lava Jato. The case triggered: (i) CVM investigations into audit quality at PwC, (ii) scrutiny of the Board's audit committee, (iii) debate about the adequacy of related-party transaction safeguards, and (iv) regulatory review of whether the Brazilian accounting standards framework adequately addressed the specific instruments used to obscure the liability.

**2024–2025 — CVM Governance Regulatory Modernization**
Following Americanas, the CVM initiated several regulatory consultations: enhanced requirements for audit committee qualifications and independence, stricter rules on management self-dealing in credit transactions, and discussion of whether Novo Mercado bylaws should explicitly require forensic accounting provisions. B3 launched a working group on governance of governance, examining whether the tier system itself needs structural reform.

### III. Governance Failure Case Studies

#### Case Study 1: Americanas S.A. (AMER3) — 2023

**Background:**
Americanas S.A. was one of Brazil's largest retailers, operating the Lojas Americanas brick-and-mortar chain and B2W/Americanas e-commerce operations. The company was listed on Novo Mercado — the highest governance tier — and counted among its controlling shareholders Jorge Paulo Lemann, Marcel Telles, and Carlos Alberto Sicupira (collectively "3G Capital founders"), three of Brazil's most prominent businesspeople.

**The failure:**
On January 11, 2023, Americanas disclosed to the market that newly appointed CEO Sérgio Rial had identified BRL 20 billion in "accounting inconsistencies." By end of January, that figure had grown to BRL 43 billion, representing one of the largest accounting frauds in Brazilian corporate history.

The mechanism involved a practice called "risco sacado" (supply chain financing): suppliers accepted early payment from banks, and Americanas recorded the bank obligations as accounts payable (trade payables) rather than as financial debt. This kept BRL 20+ billion off the financial debt line, artificially depressing the company's apparent leverage and improving interest coverage ratios for years. The scheme exploited an ambiguity in IFRS classification standards for supply chain financing arrangements.

**Governance lessons:**

1. *Board composition does not guarantee oversight quality*: Americanas's board included independent directors with financial credentials. Yet none detected or disclosed the accounting manipulation over multiple years.

2. *Audit committee effectiveness depends on genuine independence from management*: If management is sophisticated and motivated to conceal, a formalistic audit committee process — reviewing documents prepared and presented by management — may fail to detect well-designed fraud.

3. *External auditor responsibility*: PwC audited Americanas for years without qualification. The engagement raised fundamental questions about audit quality and materiality thresholds in supply chain financing arrangements.

4. *The "governance label" is not a guarantee*: Americanas's Novo Mercado listing misled some investors into believing governance risk was low. The label is a floor of minimum contractual commitments, not a ceiling on governance risk.

5. *Concentrated control creates information asymmetry*: Despite governance tier, the three controlling families had deep informal influence over senior management and board appointments, creating a principal-agent structure where genuine independence was structurally compromised.

**Market impact:**
Americanas stock fell from ~BRL 12 to near zero. Creditor losses exceeded BRL 40 billion. Banco BTG Pactual, one of the largest creditors, briefly suffered a bank run before the systemic contagion was contained by BACEN. The episode demonstrated that a single governance failure in a systemically connected company can generate financial stability risks far beyond the equity market.

#### Case Study 2: OGX Petróleo (OGXP3) — 2013

**Background:**
OGX Petróleo e Gás was the flagship oil exploration company of entrepreneur Eike Batista's EBX Group conglomerate. The company went public in 2008 in Brazil's largest IPO at the time, raising BRL 6.7 billion, and quickly became one of the Ibovespa's largest components based on a promised resource base in offshore oil blocks.

**The failure:**
OGX collapsed after repeated downward revisions to its oil reserve estimates — estimates that had been the primary justification for its BRL 150 billion peak market capitalization. The company filed for bankruptcy in October 2013, at the time the largest corporate bankruptcy in Brazilian history.

**Governance dimensions:**

1. *Founder control and board capture*: Eike Batista controlled OGX through a holding structure, maintained the CEO role, and effectively controlled the board. Independent directors provided limited check on management's optimistic reserve disclosures.

2. *Conflicts of interest in the EBX ecosystem*: OGX's related parties included OSX (shipbuilding, controlled by Batista), LLX (port logistics), and MMX (mining) — all entities where transactions could benefit the controlling shareholder at the expense of OGX minority shareholders.

3. *Disclosure of uncertain reserves as near-certainties*: OGX's public communications — including Eike Batista's personal statements — presented speculative resource estimates in language that suggested high probability of development. The gap between the communication and the geological reality was material and known internally before being disclosed publicly.

4. *Insider selling before disclosure*: Subsequent investigations revealed that management-affiliated parties reduced their exposure before key negative announcements, raising market manipulation concerns.

**Key lesson:** Companies controlled by charismatic, high-profile founders present particular governance risks when the founder's personal brand becomes conflated with the company's credibility. Board independence is most critical precisely in these cases, where social and reputational dynamics make board member dissent uncomfortable.

#### Case Study 3: Oi S.A. (OIBR3/OIBR4) — Telecom Governance Failure (2016–2022)

**Background:**
Oi S.A. is Brazil's fourth-largest telecommunications operator, providing fixed-line, mobile, and broadband services. The company filed for bankruptcy protection (recuperação judicial) in June 2016 — at BRL 65 billion in liabilities, the largest bankruptcy filing in Brazilian corporate history at the time.

**The governance failure:**
Oi's collapse was driven by a toxic combination of factors: (i) overleveraged acquisitions (particularly of Portugal Telecom's Brazilian assets in 2014), (ii) a complex shareholding structure involving Pharol (Portuguese holding company), Telemar (Brazilian holding) and Oi, with interests not perfectly aligned; (iii) related-party transactions between Oi and its related entities at non-arm's-length terms; (iv) management's repeated failure to disclose the severity of the liquidity crisis to the market.

**Key governance dimensions:**

1. *Cross-shareholding complexity hides control*: Oi's complex pyramidal ownership structure made it difficult for outside investors to identify the ultimate controlling party and assess related-party transaction arms-length nature.

2. *Board composition in multi-shareholder structure*: With multiple large shareholders (Pharol, La Vivendi, Brazilian pension funds), the board became a negotiating arena among large shareholders rather than a genuine oversight body for all equity holders, including minorities.

3. *Credit disclosure failure*: Brazilian regulations require disclosure of material facts affecting the company's financial condition. Oi management delayed material disclosure of its deteriorating liquidity position, depriving market participants of information critical for investment decisions.

4. *Restructuring process and minority impact*: The recuperação judicial process involved multiple contested restructuring plans, with different creditor classes (bondholders, trade creditors, banks) and equity holders pursuing conflicting interests. Minority shareholders were effectively subordinated and ended up with minimal recovery — a pattern predicted by strong governance analysts years before the formal default.

**Key lesson:** Complex group structures and cross-shareholdings create opacity that standard governance tier compliance cannot adequately address. Advisors must map the full corporate group structure and understand the economic and voting interests at each level.

#### Case Study 4: Samarco / Vale / BHP — Environmental Governance Failure (2015)

**Background:**
Samarco Mineração is an iron ore pelletizing company jointly owned by Vale (50%) and BHP Billiton Brasil (50%). In November 2015, a tailings dam at Samarco's Germano complex in Mariana (Minas Gerais) collapsed, killing 19 people, destroying the Doce River watershed, and creating Brazil's worst environmental disaster to date. The estimated remediation liability exceeded BRL 50 billion.

**Governance dimensions:**

1. *Joint venture governance gaps*: Samarco's 50/50 joint venture structure created shared governance responsibility but ambiguous accountability. Neither Vale nor BHP had full operational control, yet both were exposed to the full liability as sponsors of the venture.

2. *Safety governance as a board-level risk*: The dam's risk was identified in internal engineering reports prior to the collapse, but the information did not reach board level in a manner that triggered decisive action. The failure illustrated that operational safety risk must be a board-level governance matter, not merely an operational management issue.

3. *Liability cascade to parent companies*: The collapse devastated Vale's share price (at that time already under pressure from iron ore prices) and created years of litigation for BHP in Brazil, the U.S., and Australia. The event demonstrated that environmental governance failures at a controlled or jointly-controlled entity directly impact the governance risk profile of listed parent companies.

4. *ESG acceleration*: Samarco (and subsequently Brumadinho in 2019, where a Vale-operated dam collapsed killing 270 people) catalyzed a structural shift in how Brazilian institutional investors approach environmental and safety governance. Vale is now required to maintain a dedicated board-level safety committee, a requirement subsequently adopted as best practice across the Brazilian mining sector.

**Key lesson:** Governance failures in operational safety and environmental management can dwarf the financial cost of any governance compliance weakness. Decade's analysis of mining and industrial companies explicitly includes dam safety risk, environmental license status, and operational safety governance as formal risk assessment criteria.

### IV. Minority Shareholder Activism in Brazil

#### History and Evolution

Brazilian minority shareholder activism has a shorter history than in the United States, where proxy fights and activist hedge funds have operated since the 1980s. Brazil's activism landscape evolved through several phases:

**1990s — Early Advocacy Phase:**
AMEC (Associação de Investidores no Mercado de Capitais), founded in 1991, was the first organized voice for minority shareholders in Brazil. Initially focused on advocacy and policy engagement with the CVM and Bovespa, AMEC developed the first structured minority investor voting guidelines for Brazilian companies.

**2000s — Arbitration and Institutional Investor Engagement:**
The establishment of CAM (Câmara de Arbitragem do Mercado) in 2001 created the first efficient dispute resolution mechanism for governance conflicts. The first wave of institutional arbitration cases — largely regarding tag-along rights violations and material fact disclosure — established precedents that shaped subsequent corporate governance practice.

**2010s — Lava Jato and Governance Awakening:**
The Lava Jato investigation dramatically elevated governance awareness among Brazilian pension funds (ABRAPP members — Previ, Funcef, Petros) and foreign institutional investors. These large shareholders began exercising voting rights more actively, commissioning independent governance analyses, and engaging directly with board members — practices that had been rare before.

**2020s — Digital AGMs and Retail Activism:**
Digital participation in AGMs, normalized from 2020, created a new class of retail activist shareholders. While individual retail investors hold small stakes, their aggregate voting in matters affecting millions of small investors (poison pill triggers, shareholder-unfriendly capital raises) became material. Cases where retail shareholder votes influenced AGM outcomes — previously essentially unknown in Brazil — became more common.

#### Tools Available to Minority Shareholders

**1. Shareholder Meeting Voting:**
Any shareholder holding more than 5% of total shares (or lower thresholds for specific resolution types) can submit proposals for inclusion in the AGM agenda. The CVM's electronic voting platform (portalbvmf.bmfbovespa.com.br/acionistas) enables remote voting participation. Block-holder coalitions (multiple minority holders acting together through voting agreements) can achieve the 5% threshold more readily.

**2. Request for Extraordinary General Meeting (AGE):**
Shareholders holding at least 5% of total capital can convene an Extraordinary General Meeting to vote on specific matters. This is a powerful tool to force the agenda when the controlling shareholder refuses to bring a matter to a regular AGM.

**3. Fiscal Board (Conselho Fiscal) Election:**
Minority shareholders (both ON and PN holders separately) have the right to elect at least one member of the Fiscal Board when it is activated. The Fiscal Board has legal powers to examine the company's accounts, review management acts, and report directly to the AGM — providing an independent monitoring mechanism that cannot be blocked by the controlling shareholder.

**4. Arbitration (CAM):**
For companies listed on Nível 2 or Novo Mercado, any minority shareholder can initiate arbitration proceedings at B3's Câmara de Arbitragem do Mercado to enforce governance rights. CAM arbitration is faster and more specialized than court litigation — typical timeline of 18–24 months versus 7–10 years for court cases. The confidentiality of arbitration proceedings is a limitation (precedents are not publicly available), but this is partially addressed by post-proceeding summaries that B3 publishes.

**5. CVM Complaints and Investigations:**
Minority shareholders can file formal complaints with the CVM alleging securities law violations (insider trading, failure to disclose material facts, fraudulent disclosure). CVM investigations have led to administrative penalties, fines, and in severe cases, temporary bans from public capital market activity. Notable CVM enforcement actions include cases against Petrobras management for delayed Lava Jato disclosure and against fund managers for breach of fiduciary duty.

**6. Collective Action (Class Actions in Brazil):**
Brazilian law provides for collective civil actions (ação civil pública) that can be brought by investor associations on behalf of a class of affected shareholders. While less developed than U.S. securities class actions, Brazilian securities collective actions have become more active post-Americanas and post-Samarco.

#### Landmark Minority Shareholder Cases

**Telemar / Oi tag-along dispute (2012–2014):** Minority shareholders of Telemar Participações disputed the tag-along price offered in the reorganization of the Oi group, arguing it was below the economic value of the control block being transferred. The case went to CAM arbitration and established important precedent on how tag-along price should be calculated in complex multi-tier corporate reorganizations.

**Vale dam-related investor litigation (2019–2024):** Following the Brumadinho disaster, international institutional investors — including U.S. pension funds and Dutch asset managers — filed claims in multiple jurisdictions against Vale for inadequate environmental risk disclosure. The settlement reached in 2021 (approximately BRL 37 billion in total compensation across all stakeholders) was, in part, influenced by the credible threat of investor litigation.

### V. Related-Party Transaction Analysis: Identification and Evaluation

#### 5.1 Where to Find RPT Disclosure

Brazilian CVM regulations require disclosure of all material related-party transactions in:

- **Formulário de Referência (Annual Reference Form):** Section 16.1 provides a detailed table of all related-party transactions in the most recent fiscal year, including: parties involved, nature of the relationship, description of the transaction, amount, terms, and whether the board concluded the transaction was at arm's length.
- **ITR and DFP financial statements:** Note disclosures on related-party transactions as required by CPC 05 (Brazilian equivalent of IAS 24).
- **Material Facts (Fatos Relevantes):** For RPTs exceeding materiality thresholds defined in the company's bylaws or B3 rules, a separate material fact must be filed.

#### 5.2 Red Flags in Related-Party Transactions

Advisors should scrutinize the following patterns as potential indicators of value transfer to controlling shareholders:

- **Real estate transactions:** Sale or purchase of properties between the company and entities controlled by the controlling family. The valuation basis (independent appraisal? which appraiser? instructed by whom?) is critical. Below-market sales to related parties are a classic value-transfer mechanism.

- **Consulting and management service agreements:** Payments by the listed company to entities controlled by the controlling shareholder for "strategic advisory," "management services," or "brand licensing." These fees should be benchmarked against market rates for equivalent services.

- **Financing arrangements:** Loans from the listed company to related parties (or vice versa) at below-market interest rates. In Brazil, the TJLP or CDI rate is the natural benchmark for intra-group financing. Loans at significantly below CDI suggest implicit subsidization of the related party at the expense of the company.

- **Supply and distribution agreements:** Contracts where the listed company is required to sell to or buy from related parties at specified prices. If the terms are not demonstrably arm's length, value can be extracted through pricing that favors the related party.

- **Insurance arrangements:** Companies that obtain insurance through related-party brokers or insurance companies controlled by the founding family at above-market premiums.

#### 5.3 Evaluating Whether a RPT Is at Arm's Length

The key question is whether the transaction would have been available to an unrelated third party on the same terms. Indicators of genuine arm's-length basis:

1. The transaction was competitively tendered with multiple bidders.
2. An independent committee of the board (composed of directors without a conflict in this specific transaction) reviewed and approved the transaction.
3. An independent fairness opinion from a qualified financial advisor was obtained.
4. The transaction price is within the range of comparable third-party transactions in the market.
5. Minority shareholders were informed through a material fact before the transaction was completed.

### VI. Board Composition Analysis: Beyond the Independence Threshold

#### 6.1 Independence Criteria Under Brazilian Rules

B3's definition of an independent director (for Nível 2 and Novo Mercado) excludes individuals who:
- Are or have been an employee or officer of the company or a related entity in the past 3 years
- Are a direct family member of the controlling shareholder or management
- Receive compensation from the company beyond the standard director fee
- Are a significant supplier or customer of the company
- Have material direct or indirect equity interest in the company exceeding 10% (unless the stake was acquired solely as a financial investment)

**Beyond the letter:** Decade's analysis applies a stricter "spirit of independence" test. We examine: shared board memberships (directors who sit together on other boards, creating social obligation networks), charitable organization overlaps (common in Brazil's social elite, where controlling families fund charities where directors participate), and past professional relationships (former co-workers at banks, consulting firms, or government agencies who may maintain loyalty networks).

#### 6.2 Committee Structure Assessment

Effective corporate governance requires functional committees, not merely nominal ones. Decade assesses:

**Audit Committee:**
- Does it include at least one financial expert (CPA, CFO experience, or equivalent)?
- Does it meet at least quarterly?
- Does it have a direct reporting line to the external auditor, bypassing management?
- Does it review internal audit reports independently?

**Compensation Committee:**
- Is the CEO excluded from compensation committee meetings when his own compensation is discussed?
- Are compensation benchmarks derived from independent peer group analysis (not commissioned by management)?
- Is variable compensation clearly linked to pre-defined and measurable performance criteria?

**Risk Committee:**
- Does it cover operational, financial, and ESG risks?
- Does it include board members with relevant operational expertise (not only finance)?

### VII. Decade's Internal Governance Scoring Framework

Beyond listing level, Decade applies a proprietary governance scoring methodology to all equity holdings and prospective investments. The framework evaluates five dimensions on a scale of 1 to 5 (1 = poor, 5 = excellent):

**Dimension 1 — Share Structure and Minority Rights (weight: 25%)**
- Evaluates: single-class vs. dual-class shares, tag-along comprehensiveness, poison pill fairness, absence of super-voting mechanisms.
- Novo Mercado single-class: 5. Tradicional with concentrated control and no tag-along on PN: 1.

**Dimension 2 — Board Effectiveness (weight: 25%)**
- Evaluates: independence of directors (applying spirit test, not just letter), committee structure and functionality, CEO/Chair separation, board diversity, director expertise relative to company's key risks.
- Strong Novo Mercado company with 40% independent board, functional committees: 4–5. Family-dominated board in Tradicional company with nominal independent: 1–2.

**Dimension 3 — Transparency and Disclosure Quality (weight: 20%)**
- Evaluates: quality and completeness of Reference Form disclosures, earnings release transparency (segment detail, non-recurring item treatment), related-party transaction disclosure granularity, management guidance credibility (history of meeting guidance).
- Companies with consistent, proactive disclosure that exceeds regulatory minimums: 4–5.

**Dimension 4 — Capital Allocation and Dividend Policy (weight: 20%)**
- Evaluates: historical track record of capital allocation (acquisitions at fair prices? successful integrations?), dividend policy consistency (no arbitrary cuts), absence of value-destructive controlling shareholder transactions.
- Companies with 10-year track record of disciplined capital allocation and consistent shareholder distributions: 4–5.

**Dimension 5 — Alignment of Interests (weight: 10%)**
- Evaluates: management equity ownership aligned with minority interests, long-term compensation vesting, absence of excessive related-party perquisites.
- Management with significant long-duration equity ownership and clear pay-for-performance alignment: 4–5.

**Total Governance Score:** Weighted average of five dimensions, 1–5 scale. Decade does not invest in companies with total score below 2.5 in our core equity portfolios. Companies scoring 4.0–5.0 receive a positive governance premium in our valuation framework (lower WACC); companies scoring 2.5–3.0 receive a governance discount (higher WACC, typically +100 to +200 bps).

### VIII. Proxy Voting Guidelines

Decade votes its shares in all AGMs and EGMs for companies in client equity portfolios. Our voting guidelines cover the most common categories of shareholder resolutions:

**Board of Directors Elections:**
- Vote FOR candidates who meet the spirit-of-independence test as defined in our framework.
- Vote AGAINST candidates who represent a fourth or subsequent consecutive term as "independent" (tenure exceeding 10 years compromises practical independence).
- Vote AGAINST the slate where the board includes no women or no members with direct operational expertise in the company's key risk areas.

**Executive Compensation:**
- Vote FOR compensation plans where variable pay is tied to at least two independently auditable financial metrics.
- Vote AGAINST compensation plans that include guaranteed bonuses unlinked to performance, excessive perquisites, or re-pricing of underwater options without shareholder approval.

**Capital Raises and New Share Issuances:**
- Vote FOR pre-emptive rights preserving issuances where the price is at or above net asset value.
- Vote AGAINST dilutive issuances below NAV or below the undisturbed market price that benefit related parties.

**Related-Party Transactions:**
- Vote FOR RPTs that followed a demonstrably arm's-length process (competitive tender, independent committee review, fairness opinion).
- Vote AGAINST RPTs where the process is unclear, the pricing basis is not disclosed, or independent directors abstained rather than approved.

**Poison Pills:**
- Vote AGAINST poison pill adoption or renewal where the trigger threshold is below 25% (punitive deterrents to large long-term shareholders with legitimate interests).
- Vote FOR poison pills with fair-price provisions and sunset clauses requiring periodic shareholder renewal.

**Amendments to Bylaws:**
- Vote FOR bylaw amendments that improve minority shareholder protections.
- Vote AGAINST bylaw amendments that entrench management, reduce free float requirements, or curtail access to arbitration.

### IX. International Governance Comparison: Brazil vs. Peers

| Governance Dimension | Brazil (Best Practice / Novo Mercado) | United States (NYSE/SEC) | European Union (ESMA / national codes) | LatAm Peers (Mexico, Chile, Colombia) |
|----------------------|---------------------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Mandatory single-class shares | Yes (Novo Mercado) | No (dual-class common in tech) | Varies (Germany allows, UK generally no) | No — dual-class common |
| Minimum independent directors | 20% (B3 rule) | Majority independent (NYSE/Nasdaq) | Typically 30–50% per national codes | 25–40% (varies by country) |
| Mandatory audit committee | Yes (Novo Mercado, activated fiscal board or committee) | Yes (SOX requirement for public companies) | Yes (EU Audit Directive) | Varies — generally not mandatory |
| Say-on-pay (shareholder vote on exec compensation) | Not mandatory (disclosure required) | Advisory say-on-pay vote required (Dodd-Frank) | Binding or advisory depending on jurisdiction | Not standard |
| Minimum free float | 30% (Novo Mercado) | No federal minimum; exchange listing standards vary | Generally 25% for listing eligibility | 20–25% typically |
| Dispute resolution | CAM mandatory arbitration (Novo Mercado/Nível 2) | State courts (Delaware); class action viable | National courts; cross-border ECHR | Local courts; arbitration rare |
| Related-party transaction approval | Independent committee recommended; disclosure required | Audit committee approval; proxy disclosure | Varies; MiFID II oversight for financials | Disclosure-based; approval varies |
| Stewardship code | AMEC guidelines (voluntary) | Dodd-Frank; SEC investment advisors | EU Shareholder Rights Directive II (binding) | Limited formal stewardship frameworks |
| Governance index | IFIX, IGC (B3) | MSCI USA ESG leaders; S&P 500 ESG | Eurostoxx ESG, DAX ESG | Varied, limited specific governance indices |
| Whistleblower protection | Limited formal protections (improving) | SEC whistleblower program (substantial rewards) | EU Whistleblower Directive (binding) | Very limited in most markets |

**Key comparative observations:**

Brazil's Novo Mercado is genuinely competitive with the highest governance standards globally for the specific dimension of share structure (mandatory single-class common shares). The U.S. market, by contrast, has moved toward expanded dual-class share adoption in technology IPOs — a governance regression by international standards that the Brazilian Novo Mercado explicitly avoids.

However, Brazil lags significantly in: (i) board independence (20% minimum vs. majority independence in the U.S.); (ii) say-on-pay (the U.S. Dodd-Frank requirement for advisory say-on-pay is more robust than Brazil's disclosure-only approach); and (iii) whistleblower protection frameworks.

### X. Future of Governance in Brazil

**Trend 1 — ESG Integration into Listing Standards:**
B3 and the CVM are actively developing ESG disclosure requirements that would complement the existing governance tier structure. Mandatory climate risk disclosure (aligned with TCFD — Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) is under consultation, with expectation of phased implementation for large-cap companies from 2026. Companies that proactively adopt TCFD-aligned reporting are building a competitive advantage for international institutional capital allocation.

**Trend 2 — Digital Voting Expansion:**
The regulatory framework for fully digital shareholder meetings and remote voting continues to evolve. B3's digital voting platform is expected to expand to allow real-time participation (not just pre-meeting voting). This will further increase retail shareholder voting participation and create greater accountability pressure on management and boards.

**Trend 3 — Audit Quality Reform:**
Post-Americanas, the CVM and Brazil's Federal Accounting Council (CFC) are engaged in a comprehensive reform of audit standards, mandatory rotation of audit firms, and auditor liability for failures to identify or report material misstatements. The mandatory rotation every 10 years for public companies was already established; the Americanas case is likely to shorten this to 5–7 years for the largest issuers.

**Trend 4 — Gender Diversity on Boards:**
CVM Resolution 44/2021 required enhanced gender diversity disclosure. IBGC data indicates that women held approximately 16% of board seats in listed Brazilian companies as of 2024 — up from 8% in 2018 but still significantly below European regulatory targets (40% minimum in the EU for large companies under the Women on Boards Directive). Market pressure and governance scoring agencies are accelerating improvement.

**Trend 5 — Governance in Capital Markets Regulation:**
The CVM's regulatory modernization program — including the rollout of Resolution CVM 175/2022 and subsequent sectoral normatives — reflects a systemic effort to align Brazilian capital markets regulation with international best practices. This regulatory convergence benefits foreign institutional investors, reduces compliance complexity for cross-listed companies, and should continue to compress Brazil's governance discount relative to developed market peers over the next decade.
