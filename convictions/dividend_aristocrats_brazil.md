# Dividend Aristocrats of Brazil: Building a Sustainable Income Portfolio on B3

**Decade Investment Research | Conviction Document**

## Executive Summary

The concept of the "dividend aristocrat" — a company with a long, consistent, and ideally growing dividend history — translates meaningfully to the Brazilian market, albeit with important structural adaptations. Unlike the US S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats index (which requires 25+ consecutive years of dividend growth), the Brazilian market demands a different framework: shorter track records, the unique role of JCP (Juros sobre Capital Próprio) as a tax-efficient distribution mechanism, the interaction of dividends with elevated Selic rates, and the concentrated sectoral nature of high-yield Brazilian names (utilities, banks, telecoms, oil).

For income-focused clients at Decade — retirees living off portfolio distributions, wealth preservation mandates, or clients seeking to reduce equity risk without abandoning equity returns — dividend-oriented Brazilian equities deserve dedicated analysis and potentially meaningful portfolio weight. This document provides the framework for identifying genuinely sustainable dividend payers, distinguishing them from dividend traps, and constructing a portfolio calibrated to generate consistent, growing real income over a multi-year horizon.

## What Makes a Dividend Aristocrat in the Brazilian Context?

In the US context, dividend aristocracy is defined by longevity — 25 consecutive years of dividend increases, embedded in the S&P 500 Dividend Aristocrats methodology. This threshold is unreachable for most Brazilian companies, given the country's economic volatility history: the hyperinflation of the 1980s and early 1990s, the 1999 BRL devaluation, the 2002 pre-election crisis, the 2015–2016 severe recession, and the 2020 pandemic shock all created genuine discontinuities in corporate earnings and dividend policies.

A more appropriate Brazilian definition of a dividend aristocrat encompasses:

**Track record**: Consistent dividend payments (in BRL terms) for at least 7–10 consecutive years without omission.

**Yield meaningfulness**: A dividend yield (DY) that meaningfully exceeds the inflation rate — providing real income, not merely nominal distributions.

**Payout sustainability**: Dividends supported by genuine free cash flow, not accounting-driven distributions that deplete the balance sheet.

**Predictability**: A business model (regulated monopoly, concession, financial franchise) where future earnings streams are reasonably foreseeable, enabling dividend planning for both the company and the investor.

**Governance commitment**: Management teams and controlling shareholders with a stated commitment to shareholder distributions, ideally embedded in corporate bylaws or official dividend policies.

This framework identifies a manageable universe of Brazilian names — primarily in utilities, financial services, telecommunications, and natural resources — where income investing can be pursued with institutional rigor.

## Sectors Most Likely to Produce Reliable Dividends

### Electric Utilities (Energia Elétrica)

Regulated electricity companies represent the bedrock of Brazilian income investing. Their attraction is structural: revenues are determined by regulated tariffs set by ANEEL (Agência Nacional de Energia Elétrica), which are inflation-adjusted (typically IPCA-indexed) at periodic review cycles. This creates a high degree of earnings predictability.

Key dividend-paying sub-segments:
- **Electricity distribution (distribuidoras)**: Regulated return on assets established by ANEEL in periodic rate reviews (revisões tarifárias). Companies like Energias do Brasil (ENBR3), CPFL Energia (CPFE3), and Equatorial Energia (EQTL3) operate large distribution concessions.
- **Electricity generation (geradoras)**: Particularly companies with predominantly hydroelectric capacity (which has low marginal cost) and power purchase agreements (PPAs) providing revenue certainty. Engie Brasil (EGIE3), AES Brasil (AESB3), and CEMIG Geração (consolidated within CMIG4) are prominent examples.
- **Electricity transmission (transmissoras)**: Transmission companies receive RAP (Receita Anual Permitida) — fixed, inflation-adjusted annual revenues for the life of their concessions. This is arguably the most bond-like cash flow profile in Brazilian equities. ISA CTEEP (TRPL4) and Alupar (ALUP11) are leading pure-play transmission vehicles.

**Dividend yield range**: Utility companies typically yield 6–12% on a trailing basis, with lower-risk (regulation-heavy) companies at the lower end and companies carrying more operational or leverage risk at the higher end.

### Banks and Financial Services

Brazilian large-cap banks are among the most consistent dividend payers in the market, driven by the combination of high net interest margins (enabled by Brazil's historically elevated real interest rate environment), robust credit card and fee income, and capital-return commitments to shareholders.

Itaú Unibanco (ITUB4), Banco Bradesco (BBDC4), and Banco do Brasil (BBAS3) have uninterrupted dividend and JCP distribution histories spanning multiple decades. Their payout ratios have historically been in the range of 40–60% of net income, with the remainder reinvested in balance sheet growth.

**JCP dominance**: Financial sector companies are the most prolific users of JCP as a distribution mechanism (explained in detail in a later section), driven by the high book value of bank balance sheets which maximizes the JCP deduction base.

### Telecommunications

Telecommunications companies — particularly those with high market share in mobile services — generate relatively stable, recurring revenues from subscription-based consumer relationships. Dividend attraction in this sector comes from the mature phase of capital expenditure: after large investments in 4G and now 5G network buildout, companies with strong market positions generate significant free cash flow relative to incremental capex requirements.

TIM Brasil (TIMS3) and Telefônica Brasil/Vivo (VIVT3) have maintained strong dividend track records. The sector underwent significant consolidation (Oi's bankruptcy and asset sale) that improved the competitive dynamics for remaining players.

### Oil and Gas: Petrobras (PETR3/PETR4)

Petrobras is the dominant name in Brazilian oil and gas and has, in certain periods, been one of the highest absolute dividend payers in the B3 market. However, Petrobras dividends carry unique risks that differentiate it from classic dividend aristocrats:

- **Government influence**: As a state-controlled company, Petrobras's dividend policy can be influenced by the federal government's fiscal needs and political priorities. Changes in management following elections (as seen in 2022) directly impacted dividend policy and guidance.
- **Oil price dependence**: Revenues and cash generation are directly tied to Brent crude prices, creating significant cyclicality.
- **Capital allocation debates**: Tension between high dividends and capital expenditure for exploration, production, and energy transition is a recurring governance theme.

Advisors should present Petrobras dividends as a high-yield/high-risk component of an income portfolio, not as a cornerstone, and should calibrate position sizes accordingly.

## Legal Framework: Mandatory Minimum Dividend

Brazilian corporate law (Lei 6.404/76, Art. 202) establishes a minimum mandatory dividend of 30% of adjusted net income unless the company's bylaws specify otherwise. This minimum, known as the dividendo obrigatório, applies to all publicly listed companies. Companies listed on B3's Novo Mercado segment are subject to an enhanced requirement of 35% minimum distribution as part of the differentiated governance agreement, which has been in force since the 2018 Novo Mercado regulation reform.

In practice, most Brazilian companies that are actively managed for shareholder value distribute significantly more than the statutory minimum. Companies in capital-light businesses (asset management, data services, mature utilities) frequently distribute 80–100% of net income, retaining minimal earnings. The mandatory minimum functions primarily as a floor for companies with controlling shareholders who might otherwise prefer to retain cash.

Companies may suspend dividend payments above the mandatory minimum by demonstrating to shareholders at the Annual General Meeting (AGM) that: (a) such distribution would be incompatible with the company's financial situation, and (b) the retained amount will be allocated to a specific reserve. Suspension of the mandatory minimum itself requires an even higher legal burden and is rare among listed companies.

**Retained earnings reserve (reserva de lucros)**: Brazilian law permits companies to maintain a general profit reserve (up to 20% of paid-in capital) and an optional retained earnings reserve (retaining profits for capital investment plans). These reserves allow companies to smooth distributions over business cycles without suspending dividends entirely.

## Evaluating Dividend Sustainability: The Analytical Framework

Beyond the legal minimum and historical track record, advisors must assess the fundamental sustainability of a company's dividend. Three metrics are paramount:

### 1. Payout Ratio (Índice de Distribuição)

The payout ratio measures dividends and JCP distributed as a percentage of net income:

> Payout Ratio = (Dividends + JCP) ÷ Net Income

**Interpretation**:
- Payout below 60%: Comfortable room to maintain or grow distributions.
- Payout of 60–90%: Sustainable only if earnings are stable; vulnerable to earnings declines.
- Payout above 100%: Dividends exceed net income — the company is returning more than it earns, either drawing on reserves or financed through debt. Unsustainable long-term unless one-off in nature.

Note: Accounting net income in Brazil may include non-cash items (revaluation gains, amortization of goodwill, mark-to-market on financial instruments) that distort the payout ratio. Always cross-check the payout ratio against free cash flow.

### 2. Free Cash Flow (FCF) Dividend Coverage

The most important sustainability metric. Free cash flow = Operating cash flow − Capital expenditure. The FCF payout ratio:

> FCF Payout = (Dividends + JCP) ÷ Free Cash Flow

A FCF payout ratio sustainably above 100% signals that dividends are being funded from capital raises, asset sales, or debt — all of which are unsustainable long-term.

For capital-intensive sectors (utilities with large ongoing capex programs), the FCF payout can temporarily exceed 100% during investment cycles, returning to lower levels as projects enter operations. Advisors must distinguish between temporary capex elevation and structurally insufficient cash generation.

### 3. Net Debt / EBITDA and Interest Coverage

Highly leveraged companies are dividend-at-risk candidates. If the balance sheet is stressed, management may face a choice between servicing debt and paying dividends — and debt service is legally senior. Key thresholds:

- **Net Debt/EBITDA below 2.0x**: Comfortable leverage; dividend sustainability not at risk from balance sheet concerns in normal market conditions.
- **Net Debt/EBITDA 2.0–3.5x**: Moderate leverage; dividends sustainable but vulnerable to earnings deterioration or refinancing stress.
- **Net Debt/EBITDA above 3.5x**: Elevated leverage; dividend sustainability requires sustained earnings stability and access to refinancing.

Additionally, check **interest coverage** (EBITDA / Interest Expense). A ratio below 3.0x suggests the company has limited buffer between operating earnings and financial obligations.

## Dividend Yield vs. Total Return: An Important Distinction

A high dividend yield is not automatically synonymous with superior investment returns. Advisors must contextualize dividend yield within the total return framework:

**Dividend Yield** = Annual Dividends Per Share ÷ Current Share Price

A high yield can result from either: (a) generous dividends (good) or (b) a declining share price denominator (potentially bad — this is the dividend trap, discussed below).

**Total Return** = Capital Appreciation + Dividends Received

Companies that pay high dividends but grow earnings slowly will underperform companies that retain earnings for high-return reinvestment, all else being equal. The relative attractiveness of dividends vs. retained earnings growth depends on the quality of reinvestment opportunities available to management.

**Decade's view**: In the Brazilian context, where corporate governance failures and capital misallocation have historically been significant value destroyers, a clear dividend policy often signals management discipline and limits the risk of value-destructive acquisitions or related-party transactions. For this reason, a preference for dividend-paying companies in Brazil carries a governance quality signal that is somewhat absent in markets with stronger shareholder protection traditions.

## Tax Treatment of Dividends

Dividends distributed by Brazilian companies to individual investors are currently **income tax exempt at the investor level** under Brazilian law (Law 9.249/1995). This tax treatment is highly favorable — dividends pass from company to individual investor with zero marginal income tax.

This exemption has been a subject of ongoing legislative debate in Brazil, with multiple reform proposals suggesting the imposition of a withholding tax on dividends distributed to individuals (historically discussed in the 10–20% range), potentially offset by a reduction in the corporate income tax rate. As of this document's date, the exemption remains in force.

**Key implications for portfolio construction**:
- The dividend tax exemption makes Brazilian equities with high payout ratios structurally advantaged for income-focused individual investors relative to fixed income instruments (which face 15% withholding on interest income for holdings above 720 days).
- If the exemption is eventually removed, the relative attractiveness of dividend stocks vs. fixed income instruments will recalibrate — advisors should monitor legislative developments closely.
- Foreign investors (non-residents) are subject to a 15% withholding tax on dividends received from Brazilian companies (or 25% for residents of tax-haven jurisdictions).

## JCP (Juros sobre Capital Próprio): The Uniquely Brazilian Dividend Mechanism

JCP (Juros sobre Capital Próprio — notional interest on equity) is a distinctively Brazilian mechanism with no direct equivalent in other major markets. Understanding it is essential for income-focused analysis of Brazilian equities.

**The mechanism**: Brazilian corporations may distribute a notional "interest" payment to shareholders calculated on the company's shareholders' equity balance, at a rate set by the Brazilian government (the TJLP — Taxa de Juros de Longo Prazo, or since 2018, the TLP). This payment is:
- **Tax-deductible for the company** at the 34% corporate income tax rate (IRPJ + CSLL for most companies).
- **Subject to 15% withholding tax at source** for individual shareholders (20% for shareholders in tax-haven jurisdictions).

**Why companies use JCP**: The tax deductibility at the corporate level makes JCP dramatically more tax-efficient than ordinary dividends for the company. By distributing BRL 100 via JCP rather than dividend, the company saves BRL 34 in corporate taxes. The shareholder pays 15% withholding on the JCP amount. Net-net, the combined economic effect is approximately 20–25% more cash reaching the combined pool (company + shareholder) compared to an equivalent taxable dividend in many jurisdictions.

**JCP limit**: The annual JCP payment to shareholders is capped at the TJLP/TLP rate applied to the company's shareholders' equity. In periods of high interest rates (and thus high TLP), companies with large equity bases can distribute significant JCP amounts.

**Practical implication**: When comparing dividend yields across Brazilian companies, advisors must look at total distributions (dividends + JCP per share) to assess income yield. Companies that use JCP extensively may show a "dividend yield" that understates their actual total distribution yield.

## Dividend Trap Warning Signs

The most dangerous feature of dividend investing is the dividend trap: a seemingly attractive yield that proves unsustainable, accompanied by capital losses that dwarf the income received. Classic warning signs:

**1. Yield significantly above sector peers without clear justification**: If a utility company yields 15% while sector peers yield 8%, the market is pricing in risk — likely earnings deterioration, regulatory risk, or balance sheet stress. The high yield reflects skepticism about sustainability, not a value opportunity.

**2. Payout ratio consistently above 90%**: Companies paying out almost all their net income have no buffer for earnings fluctuations. A single bad year translates directly into a dividend cut.

**3. Negative free cash flow with high dividends**: Companies that report accounting profits but generate negative free cash flow (common in capital-intensive businesses during expansion phases) are funding dividends from capital raises or asset disposals. This is structurally unsustainable.

**4. Rising leverage trends**: If net debt/EBITDA is trending upward while dividends remain flat or growing, the company may be using debt to fund distributions — a red flag.

**5. Dividends driven by non-recurring items**: Asset sales, insurance proceeds, or one-time accounting gains can produce elevated single-year earnings and correspondingly elevated dividends. Advisors must identify whether the earnings base is normalized or inflated.

**6. Governance concerns**: Companies with controlling shareholders who have a history of related-party transactions, opaque cash management, or inconsistent capital allocation should receive higher dividend sustainability skepticism regardless of stated policies.

## How Dividends Interact with the Selic Rate

The Selic rate — Brazil's short-term policy rate — is the central variable governing the relative attractiveness of dividend-paying equities versus fixed income alternatives. The relationship is mechanistic and important for advisors to communicate clearly to clients.

**The basic dynamic**: When the Selic is high (as it was in 2022–2023 at 13.75%, and again at 14.75%+ in 2024–2025), the risk-free rate available through Tesouro Selic (LFT) or CDB products is high. A utility company yielding 8% in dividends competes against a government-guaranteed instrument yielding 13%+. The relative attractiveness shifts toward fixed income, which is one mechanism by which high Selic rates compress equity valuations and put upward pressure on dividend yields (through lower prices, not higher payouts).

**Dividend equities as a Selic hedge**: Conversely, when the Selic cycle turns downward, dividend-paying equities benefit disproportionately. The re-rating is driven by:
- Lower discount rates increasing the present value of future distributions.
- Capital rotating from fixed income to equities in search of yield.
- Improvement in corporate earnings for interest-sensitive sectors (utilities with floating-rate debt benefit from lower rates).

**Portfolio construction timing**: Decade does not advocate pure market timing, but advisors should note that entering long-duration dividend-paying equities during peak Selic cycles (when relative yields are most favorable) has historically produced superior risk-adjusted returns compared to entry during Selic trough periods.

## Constructing a Brazilian Dividend Portfolio

A robust Brazilian dividend portfolio is built on five principles:

**1. Sector diversification**: No more than 40% of dividend income derived from a single sector. The ideal allocation spans utilities (30–40%), financials (20–30%), telecoms (15–20%), and a smaller satellite allocation to natural resources and other dividend-paying sectors.

**2. Quality over yield**: Prefer a 7% yield from a brAAA-equivalent credit quality regulated utility over a 14% yield from a company with balance sheet stress or governance concerns. The total return of the former consistently outperforms.

**3. Distribution calendar awareness**: Map out the expected distribution timeline across holdings. Most Brazilian companies pay once or twice per year; constructing a portfolio with staggered distribution dates can create a smoother income stream.

**4. JCP maximization**: Where two companies offer similar business quality, prefer the one that makes extensive use of JCP (providing corporate-level tax efficiency) over one that distributes solely through taxable dividends.

**5. Regular fundamental review**: Dividend sustainability is not static. Regulatory changes (tariff reviews for utilities), competitive dynamics (new technology disruption in telecoms), and balance sheet evolution must be monitored continuously. A semi-annual fundamental review of dividend sustainability metrics — payout ratio, FCF coverage, leverage — is the minimum standard.

## Conclusion

Brazilian dividend aristocrats offer a compelling case for income-focused investors: structural regulatory protections for utility earnings, the uniquely tax-efficient JCP mechanism, a mandatory dividend floor providing legal backstop, and an environment where governance-disciplined dividend policy correlates with superior long-run capital allocation.

The risks are real: Brazil's macroeconomic volatility can interrupt dividend streams, leverage in capital-intensive utilities creates balance sheet exposure, and the Petrobras complex adds political risk to an otherwise predictable sector. But within a diversified framework — prioritizing quality, sustainability, and sector balance over raw yield maximization — dividend-focused Brazilian equities can anchor the equity component of an income portfolio while providing natural inflation protection through IPCA-indexed revenues.

Advisors at Decade should present this not as a yield-chasing exercise but as a conviction in the superior long-run risk-adjusted returns of quality businesses that return capital consistently and predictably — the core proposition of income investing in any market.
