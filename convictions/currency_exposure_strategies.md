# Managing Dollar and Currency Exposure for Brazilian Investors

## A Comprehensive Strategy Guide

---

## Introduction

For Brazilian investors, currency exposure is not optional — it is an ever-present reality. The Brazilian real (BRL) has historically been one of the more volatile emerging-market currencies, subject to sharp devaluations during periods of global risk-off sentiment, domestic fiscal deterioration, commodity price downturns, or political uncertainty. Between 2011 and 2025, the BRL lost more than 70% of its value against the US dollar, transforming what appeared to be solid local returns into deeply negative outcomes when measured in harder currencies.

This structural dynamic creates a fundamental challenge: Brazilian investors who hold exclusively BRL-denominated assets are exposed to a single currency whose long-term trajectory has trended toward depreciation. At the same time, over-hedging or indiscriminate dollarization carries its own costs and risks, particularly when the BRL rallies.

This guide explores the key concepts, instruments, and strategic frameworks available to Brazilian investors seeking to manage their currency exposure thoughtfully — whether the goal is protection against devaluation, portfolio diversification, or access to global assets.

---

## Why Currency Exposure Matters for Brazilian Investors

### The Structural Depreciation Tendency

Brazil's currency has faced persistent depreciation pressure driven by structural factors: persistently high inflation relative to developed markets, a history of fiscal deficits, dependence on commodity exports, and recurring political instability. While individual years have seen BRL appreciation — particularly when commodity prices surge or domestic fiscal tightening is credible — the long-term trend has favored dollar-holding strategies.

### Commodity Linkage and Procyclicality

One of the important dynamics in Brazilian currency risk is that BRL often depreciates precisely when global risk appetite deteriorates — the same conditions under which equity markets fall and credit spreads widen. This procyclicality means that a purely BRL equity portfolio may suffer doubly during crises: in local currency terms from asset price declines, and in hard-currency terms from BRL devaluation. Dollar exposure can act as a natural crisis hedge.

### The Real Economy Dimension

For investors with future international obligations — education abroad, overseas travel, imported goods, or eventual emigration — the practical need for dollar exposure is direct. Ignoring FX risk in these cases is simply foregoing a relevant protection.

## Dollarization Strategies Available in Brazil

### Brazilian Depositary Receipts (BDRs)

BDRs are certificates issued by depositary institutions in Brazil that represent shares in foreign companies listed abroad. They trade on B3 in BRL, but their prices track the underlying foreign stock combined with the USD/BRL exchange rate. When the dollar appreciates against the real, BDR prices in BRL tend to rise even if the underlying stock is flat.

Since CVM's 2020 regulatory changes, BDRs became accessible to all qualified investors, and in late 2020 they were extended to retail investors with certain restrictions. BDRs offer convenient access to companies like Apple, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and major international ETFs (including S&P 500-tracking ETFs issued as BDRs on B3).

Key considerations:
- Dividends paid by the underlying company are passed through, subject to withholding taxes at the source country
- BDRs of foreign ETFs do not benefit from the individual investor BRL 20,000/month capital gains exemption
- Liquidity varies considerably; major BDRs of large-cap companies tend to have adequate secondary market liquidity
- Currency conversion costs are embedded in the spread

### ETFs with Dollar Exposure

Several ETFs listed on B3 provide direct or indirect dollar exposure:

- **IVVB11**: Tracks the S&P 500 in USD, priced in BRL. The single most widely held dollarization vehicle for Brazilian retail investors.
- **SPXI11**: Another S&P 500-linked ETF with BRL pricing.
- **NASD11**: Tracks the Nasdaq-100.
- **GOLD11**: Gold ETF, providing indirect dollar exposure given gold's USD denomination.

These ETFs combine equity market risk with currency exposure. In a scenario where US equities fall but the dollar appreciates sharply against BRL, the net BRL return may still be positive for the Brazilian investor.

### FIIs with Dollar Income

Some Fundos de Investimento Imobiliário (FIIs) generate income denominated in or linked to the US dollar, typically through:

- Logistics properties with rental contracts indexed to the dollar (common for export-oriented tenants or bonded warehouses)
- FIIs holding international real estate certificates
- FIIs investing in CRIs backed by export revenues

These instruments do not provide pure currency hedging but create a natural linkage between portfolio income and dollar performance. They carry the full set of FII risks (liquidity, real estate market, management quality) in addition to currency dynamics.

### Direct International Investment

The most complete dollarization strategy is direct investment in assets held abroad — offshore accounts, foreign brokerage accounts, international funds. For Brazilian investors, this is regulated by the Banco Central do Brasil (BCB) and subject to capital flow declarations.

Brazilians may hold assets abroad provided they comply with annual declaration requirements to the BCB (DEREX/CBE — Capitais Brasileiros no Exterior) for holdings above USD 1 million. Smaller amounts are generally unrestricted, though income and gains must be reported to Receita Federal.

## Natural Hedges and Business-Level Currency Matching

A natural hedge arises when an investor's liabilities or future obligations in a currency are matched against assets or income in the same currency, without the use of derivative instruments.

For individual investors:
- Holding dollar-denominated assets to offset future USD-denominated expenses (tuition, medical costs abroad) constitutes a natural hedge
- Exporters or business owners with dollar revenues and BRL costs have a natural long-dollar position that reduces their need for additional dollar assets

For institutional investors and corporations:
- Export revenues naturally hedge dollar-denominated debt
- Import-oriented businesses face the opposite — they are short dollars structurally and may need to hedge with futures or forwards

Understanding one's natural position before adding deliberate FX exposure prevents unintended doubling of currency risk.

## Cupom Cambial: The Key Bridging Rate

The cupom cambial is Brazil's implied dollar interest rate derived from the interaction of domestic BRL rates and the spot USD/BRL exchange rate. It represents the return in USD that a foreign investor earns by investing in BRL-denominated assets after hedging the currency back to dollars.

Mechanically, the cupom cambial is derived from:

```
Cupom Cambial ≈ (1 + Selic) / (1 + USD/BRL forward premium) - 1
```

Or more practically, it is observed in the market for Depósitos a Termo em Dólar (DDI) traded at B3 and in NDF (non-deliverable forward) markets.

The cupom cambial is crucial because:
- It determines the cost for foreigners to invest in Brazil's fixed income while hedging back to dollars
- It influences the attractiveness of carry trades into BRL
- It is a key input in covered interest parity analysis for Brazilian markets

Historically, the cupom cambial has traded well below the Selic rate, reflecting the dollar's relative "scarcity premium" and hedging costs in Brazil. When the cupom cambial compresses significantly, it signals either tighter liquidity conditions or elevated hedging demand.

## Covered Interest Parity in the Brazilian Context

Classical covered interest parity (CIP) states that the interest rate differential between two countries should equal the forward exchange rate premium or discount. In frictionless markets, arbitrage ensures this parity holds continuously.

Brazil's case is a textbook deviation from classical CIP, and one of the most studied deviations globally. Several factors cause persistent CIP violations in Brazil:

- **IOF (Imposto sobre Operações Financeiras)**: The tax on financial transactions adds friction costs to arbitrage strategies
- **Capital flow controls and bureaucratic friction**: Even where capital flows are technically unrestricted, compliance burdens create effective barriers
- **Counterparty and sovereign risk premium**: Foreign investors demand an additional return for exposure to Brazil even when nominally hedged
- **Domestic regulation on FX positions**: Banks face regulatory limits on their FX books, constraining their ability to fully arbitrage away deviations
- **Liquidity risk**: In stressed scenarios, bid-ask spreads in dollar futures widen dramatically, creating temporal deviations

The practical implication: BRL-hedged returns available to foreign investors are structurally lower than the Selic rate suggests, and the cost of hedging dollar exposure for Brazilian investors holding foreign assets is higher than simple rate differentials would imply.

## Evaluating Hedge Efficiency

When a Brazilian investor decides to hedge their foreign currency exposure back to BRL — or conversely hedges BRL assets into dollars — understanding the efficiency of that hedge is essential. A hedge that is technically sound can still destroy value if its cost exceeds the risk reduction it provides.

Hedge efficiency is typically evaluated along three dimensions:

1. **Cost**: What is the annualized cost of maintaining the hedge, expressed as a percentage of notional? This includes the carry cost embedded in the forward or futures price, plus transaction costs.

2. **Effectiveness**: How closely does the hedge instrument's price movement offset the underlying exposure? A poorly correlated instrument may be cheap but provide inadequate protection.

3. **Liquidity and Rollover Risk**: Can the hedge be maintained through periods of market stress? B3 dollar futures (code DOL and WDO) are highly liquid under normal conditions but can exhibit significant spread widening in crisis periods.

The standard framework for evaluating currency hedge efficiency in Brazilian markets is the Paridade Coberta de Juros Ajustada (PCJA), or Adjusted Covered Interest Parity model, maintained by the BCB's research department since 2016. PCJA accounts for the cupom cambial spread, IOF friction costs, and the Selic-Fed Funds differential, outputting a hedge efficiency ratio between 0 and 1. Ratios above 0.85 indicate efficient hedging conditions where the cost of protection is justified by the risk reduction achieved. The BCB publishes PCJA readings monthly as part of its Financial Stability Report annexes.

In practice, Brazilian investors evaluating hedge efficiency should also track the implied cost of protection through the futures curve, comparing the forward rate embedded in short-dated DOL contracts to their own view on fair value of the USD/BRL exchange rate. When the futures market implies a significant BRL depreciation premium — i.e., hedging dollars back to BRL is expensive — the strategic question becomes whether to accept the FX risk unhedged or pay the hedge cost.

## Hedging Costs via B3 Dollar Futures

B3 offers two primary dollar futures contracts:

- **DOL (Futuro de Dólar Comercial)**: Standard contract, USD 50,000 notional, quoted in BRL per USD 1,000. Highly liquid with tight bid-ask spreads. Settlement is financial (no physical delivery).
- **WDO (Mini Dólar)**: Smaller contract, USD 10,000 notional, suitable for individual investors and smaller portfolios.

The cost of hedging using these instruments is embedded in the basis between spot and futures prices. When the futures price is above spot (as is normally the case given positive BRL interest rates), buying futures (going long dollars in the futures market) has a negative carry cost — you are paying an implied premium for the forward dollar.

For example, if spot USD/BRL is 5.00 and the 6-month futures contract trades at 5.22, the annualized hedging cost is approximately:
```
((5.22 / 5.00) - 1) × 2 = 8.8% per annum
```

This cost roughly tracks the interest rate differential between Brazil and the US, as arbitrage keeps futures prices anchored to the covered interest parity framework (albeit imperfectly, as discussed above).

Practical hedging costs for Brazilian investors typically include:
- Futures roll costs (paying the bid-ask on each roll)
- Margin requirements (typically 3-5% of notional in BRL, earning variation margin daily)
- Brokerage commissions
- Management overhead for active hedging programs

## Offshore Investing for Brazilians: CVM and BCB Regulations

The regulatory framework for Brazilian investors holding assets abroad has been progressively liberalized since the 2000s, but compliance requirements remain important.

**BCB Requirements**:
- Brazilians holding financial assets abroad above USD 1 million must file an annual CBE (Capitais Brasileiros no Exterior) declaration with the BCB by March of the following year
- Amounts below this threshold do not require BCB declaration but must still be reported to Receita Federal for income tax purposes
- Capital outflows above certain thresholds require documentation of the transfer purpose, though retail investors generally face no restrictions on amounts below USD 300,000 per day through authorized banking channels

**CVM Regulations for Investment Funds**:
- Brazilian investment funds (regulated by CVM) may invest internationally subject to limits defined in their prospectus and CVM instructions
- Fundos Multimercado often allocate 20-40% of assets internationally under CVM rules
- The regulatory framework for international allocation by Brazilian funds underwent significant updating in recent years, with CVM Resolution 175 (effective 2023) providing clearer frameworks for international exposure

**Tax Treatment of Offshore Assets**:
- Income from offshore assets (dividends, interest) must be reported on the annual IRPF (Imposto de Renda Pessoa Física) declaration
- Capital gains from the sale of offshore assets are taxable at progressive rates (15-22.5% depending on gain size)
- The 2023 tax reform introduced changes to the taxation of offshore holdings through controlled entities (offshores), bringing automatic annual taxation of accumulated profits even when not distributed — a significant change from the prior regime where gains were only taxed upon repatriation

## Using FX to Balance a BRL-Heavy Portfolio

Most Brazilian investors naturally begin with heavy BRL exposure: local equities, Tesouro Direto, LCI/LCA, CDBs, FIIs. The question is not whether to add dollar exposure, but how much and through which instruments.

A simple framework for determining target FX allocation:

| Investor Profile | Suggested USD/International Allocation | Primary Vehicles |
|---|---|---|
| Conservative, BRL income needs | 5-10% | IVVB11, gold ETF |
| Balanced, medium-term horizon | 15-25% | BDRs, IVVB11, offshore fund |
| Growth-oriented, long horizon | 25-40% | Direct offshore, BDRs, international ETFs |
| Business owner with BRL revenues | 20-30% | Direct offshore, matching future USD expenses |

These are indicative ranges. The appropriate allocation depends heavily on the investor's specific liability structure, time horizon, tax situation, and view on BRL fair value.

## Exchange Rate Drivers: What Moves USD/BRL

Understanding what drives the USD/BRL rate helps investors make more informed decisions about when to add or reduce currency exposure:

**Commodity Prices**: Brazil is a major exporter of iron ore, soybeans, oil, and other commodities. When commodity prices rise, dollar inflows from export revenues increase, supporting BRL. The correlation between iron ore prices and BRL has historically been meaningful.

**Interest Rate Differentials**: The Selic-Fed Funds spread drives carry trade flows. When Brazilian rates are high relative to US rates, foreign investors are attracted to BRL-denominated carry trades, supporting the currency. When the differential narrows (either because Selic falls or Fed Funds rise), carry unwind pressure weighs on BRL.

**Current Account Balance**: Persistent current account deficits require capital inflows to finance them. When financing conditions tighten globally, BRL faces pressure. Brazil's current account typically runs a modest deficit, making it dependent on portfolio inflows and FDI.

**Fiscal Dynamics**: Brazil's fiscal trajectory is closely watched by currency markets. Episodes of fiscal deterioration — rising public debt ratios, doubts about the spending cap framework — have historically coincided with BRL weakness.

**Global Risk Appetite**: In risk-off environments, BRL depreciates sharply as foreign investors reduce EM exposure. This is the primary channel through which global crises (2008, 2020) translated into BRL sell-offs.

**Political Events**: Brazilian elections, policy shifts, and institutional concerns can create significant FX volatility independent of fundamentals.

## When to Hedge vs. Accept FX Risk

There is no universal answer, but a structured decision framework helps:

**Hedge when**:
- You have specific future USD obligations with a defined timeline
- The hedge cost is modest relative to the potential downside
- Your portfolio is already concentrated in global risk assets that would suffer in a risk-off environment (dollar appreciation would partially offset equity losses)
- BRL is near what you believe to be fair value or slightly overvalued

**Accept FX risk when**:
- The cost of hedging is prohibitively high (e.g., cupom cambial is narrow, implying expensive hedge costs)
- You have a long enough time horizon to absorb volatility
- Your dollar-denominated assets themselves provide diversification value through underlying economic exposure (US equities, real assets)
- BRL appears deeply undervalued relative to purchasing power parity or commodity-justified levels

**Dynamic hedging**: Some institutional investors use rules-based dynamic hedging — increasing hedge ratios as BRL appreciates toward fair value, reducing them as BRL falls. This approach aims to buy insurance when it is cheap and reduce it when it becomes expensive.

## Tax Implications of FX Gains

Currency gains for Brazilian individual investors carry specific tax treatment:

**FX gains on foreign currency cash**: Gains from the appreciation of foreign currency held in bank accounts abroad are taxed at progressive IRPF rates (up to 27.5% for amounts above R$55,976/year). An important exemption exists: FX gains on foreign currency arising from the sale of goods (e.g., selling a property abroad) may benefit from a cost basis adjustment.

**BDRs and international ETFs**: Capital gains from BDRs and ETFs are subject to the standard 15% capital gains rate for equity-type instruments. The BRL 20,000/month exemption for small gains, which applies to direct equity sales, does not apply to BDRs.

**Dollar futures (DOL/WDO)**: Gains from financial derivatives (including dollar futures at B3) are taxed at 15% (Day trade: 20%). Losses can be offset against gains from other futures instruments.

**Foreign investment funds**: The tax reform legislation introduced auto-taxation of offshore investment fund gains (the "come-cotas" equivalent for offshore), significantly changing the planning dynamics for Brazilian investors using international structures.

Careful tax planning — ideally with a qualified Brazilian tax advisor familiar with international matters — is essential for investors holding meaningful FX exposure through multiple instrument types.

---

## Conclusion

Currency management is not a peripheral concern for Brazilian investors — it sits at the core of portfolio construction. The strategic question is not whether to have dollar exposure, but how to size it, through which instruments, at what cost, and with what level of active management.

The most successful approaches combine:
- A clear-eyed assessment of the investor's natural BRL or USD position
- Awareness of the true cost of hedging through futures and the cupom cambial framework
- Instrument selection aligned with tax efficiency and liquidity needs
- A view — even a probabilistic one — on BRL fair value and its key drivers
- Periodic rebalancing as exchange rates and portfolio values shift

Currency exposure, managed thoughtfully, transforms from a passive vulnerability into an active tool for portfolio resilience.
