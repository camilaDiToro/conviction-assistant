# Insider Trading Regulations in Brazil: Framework and Practical Guidance

*Decade Investment Research | Internal Conviction Document*

---

## Overview

Brazil's insider trading regulatory framework has matured significantly over the past three decades, moving from a system with limited enforcement capacity to one with substantive investigative powers, meaningful penalties, and growing jurisprudence. For investors in Brazilian equities, understanding the regulatory landscape — including disclosure obligations, blackout periods, and how to read insider transaction filings — provides an important layer of information and context for investment decisions. This document summarizes the legal and regulatory framework and offers practical guidance for Decade's analysts.

---

## Legal Foundation

### Lei 6.385/76 — The Securities Market Law

Brazil's foundational securities regulation, Lei 6.385/76, established the CVM (Comissão de Valores Mobiliários) and created the initial framework for market regulation. Insider trading (uso indevido de informação privilegiada) was addressed broadly in this law but lacked specific definitions and clear enforcement mechanisms, limiting early CVM action.

The law was substantially updated over subsequent decades, most critically by:

**Lei 10.303/2001**: Amended Lei 6.404/76 (the Corporations Law) and Lei 6.385/76, explicitly criminalizing insider trading for the first time in Brazil. Article 27-D of the amended Lei 6.385/76 established insider trading as a criminal offense punishable by imprisonment of 1 to 5 years plus a fine.

**Lei 13.506/2017**: A watershed reform that dramatically expanded CVM enforcement powers. The key innovations:

- Created an administrative enforcement track (Processo Administrativo Sancionador — PAS) with penalties of up to R$ 500 million or three times the gain from the illegal operation (whichever is greater)
- Authorized the CVM to negotiate Term of Commitment (Termo de Compromisso) agreements — Brazil's equivalent of consent orders — allowing faster resolution without admission of guilt
- Expanded the CVM's access to financial and banking data through cooperation protocols with Banco Central do Brasil, Receita Federal, and COAF (Conselho de Controle de Atividades Financeiras)
- Established whistleblower reward provisions (discussed below)

The criminal prosecution channel runs through the Ministério Público Federal (MPF) and requires a more demanding standard of proof, resulting in the administrative PAS track being the more frequently used enforcement mechanism in practice.

## Definition of Insider Trading

Under Brazilian law, insider trading requires three elements:

1. **Privileged information (informação privilegiada)**: Non-public information that is relevant (capable of materially influencing the price of a security) and obtained by virtue of a position (director, officer, board member, shareholder, employee, auditor, or any person who came into contact with the information due to a professional, commercial, or trust relationship with the company).

2. **Trading**: Buying, selling, or otherwise dealing in securities of the company to which the information relates, or in derivatives linked to those securities.

3. **Before disclosure**: The trading occurs prior to the public disclosure of the relevant information.

The concept extends beyond traditional "corporate insiders." CVM resolutions make clear that "tippees" — individuals who receive inside information from insiders and trade on it — are also liable. The key question in tippee liability is whether the recipient knew or should have known that the information was privileged.

## Disclosure Obligations for Insiders

### Fato Relevante (Material Fact)

The Instrução CVM 358/02 (now incorporated into CVM Resolution 44/2021) governs Material Fact disclosures. Any "relevant act or fact" that "may significantly influence the price of listed securities" must be immediately disclosed to the market via the CVM's electronic filing system (EDGAR-equivalent: sistema de divulgação de informações — Fundos.net for funds, and via the company's Investor Relations portal under CVM requirements).

Material facts include but are not limited to: mergers and acquisitions (signing, closure, or termination), dividend declarations above or below guidance, revision of earnings guidance, regulatory decisions materially affecting the company, agreements or terminations of material contracts, changes in controlling shareholders, and significant judicial decisions.

The company's Chief Investor Relations Officer (Diretor de Relações com Investidores — DRI) bears primary legal responsibility for timely and accurate Material Fact disclosures. CVM has sanctioned individual DRIs in cases of late or incomplete disclosure.

### Insider Transaction Reports (Formulário de Negociações)

CVM Resolution 44/2021 requires that the following persons report all transactions in company securities within 5 business days of the transaction:

- Directors (diretores)
- Board members (conselheiros de administração e conselho fiscal)
- Controlling shareholders (acionistas controladores)
- Members of any technical or advisory committee with access to Material Facts

Reports must specify: security, quantity, price, date, and whether the transaction was direct or through a fund/vehicle. These filings are publicly available on the CVM's website and the company's IR page, providing investors with a near-real-time window into insider sentiment.

## Blackout Periods

Brazilian regulation requires companies to establish and enforce blackout periods (períodos de vedação à negociação). Under CVM Resolution 44/2021:

- A mandatory blackout applies for the 15-day period immediately preceding the disclosure of quarterly (ITR) and annual (DFP) financial results.
- Companies must establish their own "securities trading policy" (política de negociação de valores mobiliários) defining blackout periods, pre-clearance procedures, and permitted transactions (e.g., 10b5-1 equivalent plans for pre-scheduled diversification programs, called "programas de negociação" in Brazil).
- Additional blackout periods apply whenever a Material Fact has been decided upon internally but not yet disclosed (the "silent period" before a deal announcement).

Large Brazilian companies (Petrobras, Vale, Itaú) have comprehensive securities trading policies that establish rolling blackouts aligned with the earnings calendar and additional windows for deal-specific restrictions.

## CVM Enforcement Powers and Recent Cases

The CVM's Superintendência de Relações com Empresas (SEP) and Gerência de Acompanhamento de Insider Trading (GAIT) conduct systematic market surveillance, using quantitative alert systems to flag abnormal trading volumes or price movements ahead of Material Fact disclosures.

Notable CVM enforcement actions involving insider trading:

- **Sadia/Perdigão merger (2006)**: One of Brazil's earliest high-profile insider trading cases. CVM identified abnormal options activity in Perdigão shares ahead of the announced merger. The case established important precedents for CVM evidentiary standards and resulted in administrative fines.

- **Alpargatas (2013)**: Directors and related parties were sanctioned for trading before disclosure of material financial information.

- **Multiple small-cap cases (2019–2024)**: The CVM's enhanced data-sharing arrangements with B3 and COAF under Lei 13.506/17 have produced a substantial increase in enforcement actions against smaller companies, including cases involving securities transactions by family members of directors and transactions through offshore vehicles.

CVM administrative sanctions most commonly result in fines (multas) quantified as the greater of R$ 500 million, twice the value of the illegal offering, or three times the gain obtained. Repeated violations can result in disqualification from serving as officer or board member of publicly listed companies (inabilitação temporária).

## Comparison to U.S. SEC Enforcement

Brazil's insider trading regime has converged toward U.S. standards in several respects but retains meaningful differences:

| Dimension | Brazil (CVM) | United States (SEC) |
|---|---|---|
| Maximum administrative fine | R$ 500M or 3x gain | $10M or 3x gain (per violation) |
| Criminal penalty | 1–5 years imprisonment | Up to 20 years imprisonment |
| Whistleblower award | Up to 5% of fine imposed | 10–30% of sanctions over $1M |
| Tippee liability | Yes, if aware of privileged nature | Yes (Dirks/Salman standard) |
| 10b5-1 equivalent | Programas de negociação | 10b5-1 plans |
| Speed of enforcement | Slower (2–5 years typical) | Faster (1–3 years typical) |

The SEC's enforcement record is more robust in terms of volume of cases and severity of individual penalties, partly reflecting larger dedicated resources (the SEC's Division of Enforcement has approximately 1,200 staff versus CVM's total headcount of approximately 400). However, the CVM's post-Lei 13.506/17 trajectory shows accelerating enforcement activity, particularly in the Termo de Compromisso settlements that offer faster resolution.

## Whistleblower Protections

Lei 13.506/17 introduced whistleblower incentives for the first time in Brazilian securities law. Persons who report securities violations — including insider trading — to the CVM may receive between 2% and 5% of the administrative fine ultimately imposed, provided the report contains specific and verifiable information that materially contributes to the CVM's investigation.

The program remains in early stages relative to the SEC's Office of the Whistleblower (which has paid over $1.5 billion in awards since 2012). CVM whistleblower awards have been modest in scale, partly reflecting the lower awareness of the program and the still-developing culture of compliance reporting in Brazilian corporations. The government has signaled interest in strengthening whistleblower protections as part of broader anti-corruption governance reforms.

## Practical Guidance for Investors Reading Insider Transactions

Insider transaction filings in Brazil provide actionable signal for equity analysts:

**Cluster buying by multiple insiders**: When three or more directors or board members buy in the same 30-day window without corresponding major index movements, this represents strong positive insider signal. The most reliable situations are open-market purchases (not option exercises or share plan vesting) at prices above recent market levels.

**Sustained selling despite blackout compliance**: Progressive sales by a CEO or controlling family, conducted in programas de negociação but at a steady pace regardless of price level, may reflect succession planning or portfolio diversification rather than negative view — context and magnitude matter.

**Pre-clearance and programa structures**: Investors should check whether insider selling is governed by a pre-declared programa de negociação (visible in the company's securities trading policy, typically published on the IR website). Sales within a pre-approved programa are less informative as a bearish signal than discretionary open-market sales.

**CVM filing latency**: The 5-business-day reporting window creates a lag. Insiders trading early in a reporting window may be acting on information not visible to the market for up to a week. Decade monitors CVM EDGAR filings daily for all material holdings in client portfolios.

**Related-party transactions disclosure**: Material agreements between the company and its controlling shareholders or directors must be disclosed as Material Facts or in the annual Reference Form (Formulário de Referência). Cross-referencing insider transaction patterns with related-party disclosures can surface potential conflicts of interest not immediately visible in standard financial analysis.
