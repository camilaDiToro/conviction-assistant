# Family Office Structures in Brazil: A Guide for High-Net-Worth Families

**Decade Investment Research | Wealth Management**
*Updated: April 2026*

---

## Introduction

The family office model — long established in North America and Europe as the gold standard of wealth management for ultra-high-net-worth families — has undergone rapid maturation in Brazil over the past decade. The combination of wealth concentration in the upper tiers of the Brazilian economy, the increasing complexity of cross-border assets, and a regulatory environment that has progressively clarified the responsibilities and requirements of investment advisors has created fertile conditions for the growth of both single family offices (SFOs) and multi-family offices (MFOs).

For families navigating the choice between SFO and MFO structures, the decision involves trade-offs between cost, control, privacy, services breadth, and governance quality. This guide covers the operational and structural dimensions of both models in the Brazilian context, with attention to regulatory requirements, holding structures, and succession planning considerations.

## Single Family Office vs. Multi-Family Office

### Single Family Office (SFO)

A single family office serves exclusively one family — and in practice, typically one family's extended network across multiple generations and branches. The SFO is established, funded, and controlled by the family. It employs a dedicated team of professionals (investment officers, tax specialists, legal counsel, concierge service providers) and manages all aspects of the family's financial and non-financial affairs.

**Core advantages of the SFO model**:

- **Absolute confidentiality**: All information, strategies, and family circumstances remain exclusively within the family and its directly employed team. No third-party advisors or competing clients.
- **Full customization**: Investment policy, governance structure, family governance processes, philanthropy strategy, and administrative services are designed entirely around the family's specific needs.
- **Control**: The family controls the investment decision-making process, the hiring of managers and advisors, and the pace of decision-making.
- **Alignment**: SFO employees work solely for the family — there are no conflicts of interest from serving other clients.

**Cost threshold**: The operational cost of a standalone SFO (salaries, office space, technology, legal, accounting, regulatory compliance) is typically R$2-5 million per year for a lean but professional operation. This cost structure is only justifiable for families with investable assets above R$80-150 million.

### Multi-Family Office (MFO)

A multi-family office provides wealth management services — investment advisory, tax planning, estate planning, family governance, philanthropy management, lifestyle services — to multiple client families simultaneously. Costs are shared across the client base, and the professional team is larger and more specialized than most families could afford individually.

**Core advantages of the MFO model**:

- **Economies of scale**: Access to institutional-grade investment management, sophisticated tax planning, and specialized legal advisory at a fraction of the cost of replicating these services in a SFO.
- **Breadth of expertise**: Larger teams can include specialists in areas (private equity, real assets, international tax, philanthropy) that small SFO teams cannot cover adequately.
- **Investment access**: MFOs with meaningful aggregate assets under management gain access to institutional investment vehicles, private funds, and co-investment opportunities unavailable to individual families.
- **Independence from banks**: Unlike private banks, well-structured MFOs offer open architecture investment management without product distribution conflicts.

**Trade-offs**: Less confidentiality than a SFO (though reputable MFOs maintain strict information barriers), less customization, potential conflicts when multiple client families have competing interests in the same investment.

**Cost threshold**: MFO services typically make sense for families with R$20-80 million in investable assets — large enough to benefit from sophisticated services but below the threshold where a SFO is cost-effective.

## Typical Services

Both SFOs and MFOs typically cover the following service areas, though depth and customization vary:

### Investment Management

The core service: strategic asset allocation, manager selection, portfolio construction, monitoring, and reporting. Institutional-grade investment management includes:
- Strategic and tactical asset allocation
- External manager selection and monitoring (open architecture)
- Direct investment evaluation (private equity, real estate, venture capital)
- International portfolio management
- Derivatives and risk management

### Tax Planning

Brazilian tax planning for wealthy families is complex and evolving. Key areas include:
- Optimization of investment vehicle structures (holding, FIP, fund)
- Estate and succession tax planning (ITCMD optimization across states)
- IR optimization: dividend policy, JCP, tax-efficient investment vehicles
- International tax considerations for families with offshore assets or non-resident members

### Estate Planning and Succession

Succession planning in Brazil operates under civil law rules that constrain flexibility — the *legítima* principle requires that at least 50% of the estate passes to forced heirs (descendants and ascendants). Planning within these constraints involves:
- Holding company structures to aggregate assets and facilitate organized succession
- Shareholder agreements (acordos de sócios) governing governance and liquidity
- Family constitutions (protocolo de família) establishing governance principles
- Life insurance and VGBL products as non-inventory assets for rapid transfer to beneficiaries

### Family Governance

For families beyond the first generation, governance processes become critical to preserving cohesion and avoiding the conflicts that destroy family wealth. Services include:
- Family assembly facilitation
- Family council governance design
- Next-generation education programs
- Conflict resolution frameworks
- Communication protocols between family and SFO/MFO staff

### Philanthropy

Structured philanthropy is increasingly a priority for Brazilian ultra-high-net-worth families. Services include:
- Foundation (Fundação) or Association (Associação) establishment
- Impact investment structuring
- Donor-advised fund equivalents
- ESG integration in investment policy

## Regulatory Requirements in Brazil

### CVM Registration for Investment Advisors

Investment advisors managing third-party assets in Brazil must be registered with CVM as Assessores de Investimento (AI) or Gestores de Recursos (fund managers). The ICVM 558/2015 (replaced by the more recent regulatory framework) governs asset managers; ICVM 598/2018 governs investment advisors (assessores).

Family offices providing investment advice to client families occupy a regulatory middle ground:
- **SFOs** advising only the single family that owns and funds them are generally not subject to CVM registration requirements — they are not considered third-party asset managers.
- **MFOs** providing advisory services to multiple unrelated families must comply with CVM registration requirements for investment advisors or asset managers, depending on the specific services provided.

### CFP Certification

The CFP (Certified Financial Planner) certification — administered in Brazil by the Planejar association under the global CFP Board framework — is not legally required for wealth management professionals, but has become an industry standard credential. Families selecting an MFO or SFO professional team should expect senior advisors to hold CFP or equivalent credentials (CEA for XP-ecosystem advisors, CFA for investment analysts).

## Holding Structures: Onshore vs. Offshore

### Onshore Holding Structures

The predominant structure for Brazilian family wealth consolidation is an onshore holding company — a Sociedade Anônima (SA) or Sociedade Limitada (Ltda) that owns the family's investment assets, business interests, and real estate. Advantages include:

- **Succession planning**: Shares can be transferred, donated, or bequeathed more flexibly than direct asset ownership.
- **Governance**: Shareholders agreements, tag-along and drag-along rights, and board structures provide governance tools unavailable to individual direct asset holders.
- **Tax efficiency in certain scenarios**: Dividend distributions from holding to shareholders are currently exempt from IR (though this may change); retained earnings within the holding can be reinvested without triggering immediate personal tax.
- **Asset protection**: In some structures, holding company assets can be insulated from personal creditors of individual family members.

**Limitations**: ITCMD (estate and gift tax) on transfers of holding shares is assessed based on asset value, not just equity value — states use different methodologies, creating planning opportunities and complexities.

### Offshore Structures

Brazilian residents with assets abroad have long used offshore holding structures (historically in the British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands, Luxembourg, or the Netherlands). The use of offshore structures has been significantly affected by:

- **CRS (Common Reporting Standard)**: Brazil participates in automatic information exchange, eliminating banking secrecy. Undeclared offshore assets are no longer hidden from the Receita Federal.
- **Receita Federal Normative Instructions**: Mandatory disclosure of offshore assets and income in annual IRPF and DECEX filings.
- **2023 offshore taxation reform (Law 14,754/2023)**: Significantly changed the tax treatment of controlled foreign corporations (CFCs) and offshore investments. Annual accrual taxation of CFC profits at 15% replaced the previous regime for most structures.

Legitimate uses of offshore structures remain — access to international investment strategies, estate planning for non-Brazilian family members, currency diversification — but require careful compliance planning.

## Brazilian Succession Law Considerations

The Brazilian Civil Code imposes constraints on testamentary freedom that are more restrictive than common law jurisdictions:

- **Legítima**: 50% of the estate is reserved by law for forced heirs (herdeiros necessários) — descendants in ascending order of priority, then ascendants. The testator may freely dispose of only the remaining 50%.
- **ITCMD**: The state-level estate and gift tax ranges from 4% to 8% across Brazilian states (São Paulo: 4%; some states up to 8%). The Brazilian government has discussed — but not yet implemented — a progressive ITCMD with rates up to 16%.
- **Inventory process**: Deaths without a will (ab intestato) or with contested wills trigger judicial inventory proceedings that can take years and consume 6-15% of the estate in fees and taxes.

Planning tools that mitigate these constraints:

1. **Holding company with systematic gifting**: Annual transfer of holding shares to heirs within annual gift tax exemptions reduces estate subject to ITCMD at death.
2. **VGBL and life insurance**: Assets held in VGBL plans and life insurance policies pass directly to named beneficiaries outside the estate — not subject to inventory and ITCMD treatment in most states.
3. **Testamentary trust structures**: While Brazil does not have a trust law equivalent to common law jurisdictions, offshore trusts governed by foreign law (Cayman, BVI, Jersey) with Brazilian assets can be structured to provide trust-like succession mechanics — subject to recognition and tax implications.

## Integration with Pension Funds and Insurance Products

Sophisticated family office structures in Brazil routinely integrate:

- **EFPC (Closed pension funds)**: Large business families sometimes establish family EFPCs — closed pension funds serving the family and its key employees. Contributions are deductible, returns compound tax-efficiently, and benefits can be structured to complement the family's succession plan.
- **PGBL/VGBL**: As discussed in our dedicated guide, these products provide succession benefits (direct transfer to beneficiaries) and favorable long-term tax treatment. MFOs and SFOs typically optimize the placement of assets across PGBL/VGBL products for individual family members.
- **Life insurance**: High-coverage life insurance products serve double duty — providing genuine risk coverage and creating a pool of assets outside the estate for rapid transfer to beneficiaries.

## Trends in the Brazilian Market

Several structural trends are reshaping the Brazilian family office landscape:

- **Professionalization**: The first generation of Brazilian fortunes built on industrialization and real estate is transitioning to the second and third generation, creating demand for professionalized governance and investment management.
- **Technology adoption**: SFO and MFO platforms are investing in technology for consolidated reporting, portfolio analytics, and family communication.
- **Open architecture**: The shift away from captive bank product distribution toward open architecture investment management has been accelerated by the growth of independent platforms and regulatory encouragement.
- **International integration**: As more Brazilian families build international businesses and educate children abroad, demand for cross-border wealth management expertise has grown.
- **ESG and impact investing**: Particularly among next-generation family members, ESG and impact investing integration is increasingly a requirement — not an option — in investment policy statements.
