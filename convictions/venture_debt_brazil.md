# Venture Debt in Brazil: Market Overview and Investment Perspectives

**Decade Investment Research | Private Markets**
*Updated: April 2026*

---

## What Is Venture Debt

Venture debt is a form of debt financing provided to early- and growth-stage companies — typically those that have already raised equity from venture capital investors — as a complement to, not a replacement for, equity capital. Unlike traditional bank lending, venture debt providers accept higher risk profiles: portfolio companies are often pre-profitability, asset-light, and dependent on continued equity financing for survival.

The defining characteristic that distinguishes venture debt from equity is its non-dilutive structure. Founders and early investors retain their ownership percentages — a critical consideration for cap table management in multiple-round startups. In exchange for accepting credit risk that traditional banks decline, venture lenders receive a combination of interest income, fees, and typically warrants (the right to purchase a small amount of equity at a predetermined price).

In Brazil, venture debt has grown from a niche product offered by a handful of international specialists to a recognized asset class with domestic providers, regulatory familiarity, and a track record of transactions spanning fintech, healthtech, edtech, agtech, and B2B SaaS.

## How Venture Debt Differs from Equity

Understanding the investor perspective requires clarity on the structural differences:

| Dimension | Venture Debt | Equity |
|---|---|---|
| Claim priority | Senior secured creditor | Residual claimant |
| Return profile | Current income (interest) + warrant upside | Capital appreciation only |
| Dilution | Minimal (warrant coverage typically 1-5% of loan) | Significant at each round |
| Tenor | 24-48 months typically | Indefinite (until exit) |
| Covenants | Financial and operational covenants | Board control provisions |
| Default risk | Yes — lender can accelerate and enforce | No equivalent event |
| Return expectation | 20-35% IRR (Brazil market) | 25-50%+ IRR for VC funds |

Venture debt occupies a structural position senior to all equity in the capital structure, meaning lenders are paid before any equity holder in liquidation. This structural seniority, combined with covenants and security packages, provides meaningful downside protection relative to equity — though not equivalent to the protection of traditional secured lending against profitable companies.

## Typical Structures Used in Brazil

Brazilian legal and regulatory frameworks shape the specific instruments used in venture debt transactions:

### Cédula de Crédito Bancário (CCB)

The most common instrument for direct lending by financial institutions to startups. A CCB is a credit note issued by the borrower in favor of the lender, governed by Law 10,931/2004. CCBs have streamlined enforcement — the lender can execute the instrument directly in court without full litigation. They can carry fixed or floating rates (CDI+), and their terms are highly flexible. CCBs must be issued in connection with a credit operation by a financial institution; non-financial lenders use other instruments.

### Debêntures

Debt securities issued by joint-stock companies (sociedades anônimas). Debentures are more appropriate for larger, more mature companies and require CVM filing if publicly distributed. For venture debt, privately-placed debentures (Instrução CVM 476) are the typical choice — issued to qualified investors without public offering requirements. Debentures can be convertible (into equity at the lender's option) — a structure popular in pre-revenue or very early-stage transactions.

### CRI (Certificados de Recebíveis Imobiliários) and CRA (Certificados de Recebíveis do Agronegócio)

Used for startups operating specifically in real estate (proptech) or agribusiness (agtech) verticals. These instruments offer tax advantages for investors (exempt from IR for individuals) and can be structured around receivables from the startup's customer base. More complex to structure but potentially lower borrowing cost for the startup.

### Mútuo (Direct Loan Agreement)

For non-financial investors (family offices, holding companies, impact funds), a direct loan agreement (mútuo) may be used. Less enforceable than a CCB but simpler to document. Often used for bridge loans and smaller amounts while a more formal instrument is being structured.

## Who Provides Venture Debt in Brazil

### Specialized Venture Debt Funds

A small but growing ecosystem of funds dedicated to venture debt has emerged in Brazil. These funds typically raise capital from institutional LPs (family offices, pension funds, fund of funds) and deploy into portfolios of 10-20 growth-stage companies. They combine credit expertise with venture knowledge — a pairing that traditional banks and traditional VC funds typically lack.

### Fintechs and Alternative Lenders

Some credit fintechs have moved up-market from SME lending into venture debt, leveraging their underwriting technology to evaluate growth metrics (ARR, MRR, retention, NPS) alongside traditional financial indicators. Companies like Monkey Exchange (now Nasdaq-listed), Creditas (at various stages), and specialized platforms have provided debt to growth companies.

### Traditional Banks (Limited Participation)

Major Brazilian banks (Itaú BBA, BTG Pactual, Bradesco BBI) participate selectively in growth company financing, typically at later stages with clearer paths to profitability. Their participation in early-stage venture debt is limited by regulatory capital requirements (Basel III risk weighting of unsecured startup loans is punitive) and internal risk appetite.

### International Venture Lenders

Several global venture debt providers have established Brazilian operations or participate in transactions via local partners: Silicon Valley Bank (now part of First Citizens/SVB), Kreos Capital, Western Technology Investment, and TriplePoint Capital have participated in Brazilian transactions, particularly for startups with international investor backing.

## Typical Terms

Brazilian venture debt transactions as of 2026 show the following typical parameters:

- **Loan amount**: R$2 million to R$50 million, with most transactions in the R$5-20 million range
- **Tenor**: 24 to 48 months
- **Interest rate**: CDI + 6% to CDI + 14% per year, depending on credit quality, stage, and collateral
- **Origination fee**: 1-3% of the loan amount
- **Warrant coverage**: 1-5% of the loan amount in equity warrants, exercisable at the price of the most recent funding round
- **Draw periods**: Some facilities are structured as revolving or term facilities with staged draws, tied to revenue or operational milestones
- **Covenants**: Typical covenants include minimum cash balance maintenance, minimum revenue growth thresholds, limitations on new senior debt without lender consent, and change-of-control provisions

Interest rates in Brazilian venture debt are substantially higher than in the US or Europe, reflecting Brazil's high base rate (CDI), structural credit risk premiums, and the nascency of the market. A startup paying CDI + 10% at a CDI of 13% is paying approximately 23% per year — a meaningful cost that must be justified by the use of proceeds.

## When Startups Use Venture Debt

### Between Equity Rounds

The primary use case. A startup that raised a Series A round and expects to raise a Series B in 18-24 months can use venture debt to extend its runway without accepting the dilution that would come from an interim equity raise. Venture debt effectively "buys time" for the company to hit milestones that justify a higher valuation in the next round.

### Working Capital and Revenue Financing

SaaS and subscription companies with predictable, recurring revenue can use venture debt for working capital — funding sales and marketing spend against future contracted revenue. Revenue-based financing (a variant where repayment is a percentage of monthly revenue rather than fixed debt service) is growing in Brazil, though regulatory treatment remains less defined.

### Capital Equipment and Infrastructure

Hardware, IoT, and logistics startups with physical assets can use venture debt for capex financing. The asset base provides security that reduces lender risk and can lower the cost of debt.

### Pre-IPO Bridge

More mature startups in the 12-18 months before an expected public offering sometimes use venture debt as bridge financing, avoiding a late-stage equity round at potentially unfavorable valuations.

## Investor Perspective: Risk and Return

### Return Profile

Brazilian venture debt funds target gross returns of 20-30% IRR, with the return composed of:
- Interest income (the dominant component, providing current cash yield)
- Origination and other fees
- Warrant appreciation (the "equity kicker" — highly variable, often zero if the startup fails to appreciate)

The interest income component provides a degree of return visibility unusual in venture investing — lenders receive regular cash flows regardless of the startup's equity valuation trajectory.

### Risk Profile

The key risks for venture debt investors:

1. **Default risk**: Startups fail at high rates. When a venture-backed company defaults on its debt, the recovery process for the lender is more complex than traditional secured lending — assets may be intangible, the company may have burned cash rapidly before default, and insolvency proceedings in Brazil can be protracted under the Recuperação Judicial framework.

2. **Subordination risk**: If the company has prior senior creditors (BNDES, development bank facilities) or if new senior facilities are incurred, the venture lender may find its position structurally subordinated despite contractual protections.

3. **Covenant enforcement risk**: Enforcing covenants in distressed scenarios requires legal capacity, willingness to trigger insolvency proceedings, and reputational tolerance for conflict with portfolio companies and their VC sponsors.

4. **Warrant dilution risk**: Warrants are valuable only if the company appreciates substantially. In base cases where companies grow moderately or fail, warrant income is zero.

### Risk-Adjusted Return vs. Equity

Venture debt occupies a compelling risk-return position between traditional credit (lower risk/return) and VC equity (higher risk/return). Default rates for venture debt portfolios diversified across 15-20 companies are typically 5-15% of positions, with recoveries varying widely. Portfolios with strong underwriting and structuring discipline have generated 18-25% net IRRs in the Brazilian market.

## Regulatory Treatment

Venture debt in Brazil operates across multiple regulatory spaces:

- **CCBs and direct loans by financial institutions**: Regulated by BACEN. Capital requirements under Basel III apply.
- **FIDC (Fundo de Investimento em Direitos Creditórios)**: A regulated vehicle for securitizing venture receivables. Subject to CVM instruction and rated by credit rating agencies.
- **Debenture issuances (private)**: Subject to CVM Instrução 476 for private placements to qualified investors. Lighter disclosure requirements than public offerings.
- **Equity warrants**: Regulated by CVM as securities. Must be registered and may trigger reporting obligations.

Non-financial investors providing venture debt must use instruments available to non-banks (mútuo, debentures, convertible notes) and cannot issue CCBs or accept deposits.

## Market Size and Maturity

The Brazilian venture debt market remains small relative to Brazil's venture capital ecosystem — estimated at R$1-3 billion in annual origination as of 2025, versus over R$10 billion in annual VC equity deployed in peak years. Key characteristics of market maturity:

- The legal and regulatory framework for venture debt instruments is well-established.
- Awareness among founders has grown significantly — 3-5 years ago, many Brazilian startup CEOs were unfamiliar with venture debt as a concept; today, it is routinely discussed in fundraising conversations.
- The number of dedicated providers remains small (fewer than 10 specialized funds), limiting competition and maintaining pricing discipline.
- The historical default data set is still thin — Brazil has not experienced a full credit cycle with significant venture debt volume, making statistical risk modeling imprecise.
